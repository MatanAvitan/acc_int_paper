\section{Method: \TanGCE{}}
\label{sec:method}

We now introduce \emph{Tangent Gradient-based Concept Erasure} (\TanGCE{}), the method presented in this paper. The method is built from the geometric background above: if the representation support is locally approximated by an embedded manifold $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, then the ambient erasure gradient should be edited so that the update moves along the tangent space $T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M}$ rather than along directions normal to the manifold.

\subsection{Local tangent estimation}

For each query point $\mathbf{x}_i$, we estimate the tangent space $T_{\mathbf{x}_i}\mathcal{M}$ via local PCA on its $k$-nearest neighbors. Let $\mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x}_i) = \{\mathbf{x}_{j_1}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_{j_k}\}$ denote the $k$ nearest neighbors of $\mathbf{x}_i$ in the training set. We form secant vectors $\mathbf{s}_{j_m} = \mathbf{x}_{j_m} - \mathbf{x}_i$ and compute the local covariance:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:local_cov}
\mathbf{C}_i = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{m=1}^{k} \mathbf{s}_{j_m} \mathbf{s}_{j_m}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}.
\end{equation}
The top $r$ eigenvectors of $\mathbf{C}_i$ form the tangent basis
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_basis}
\mathbf{B}_i = [\mathbf{b}_i^{(1)}, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_i^{(r)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r},
\end{equation}
where the intrinsic dimension $r$ is selected automatically by a retained-variance rule. The resulting orthogonal projector onto the estimated tangent space is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_proj}
\mathbf{P}_{T_i} = \mathbf{B}_i \mathbf{B}_i^\top.
\end{equation}

This is the empirical analogue of the differential-geometric tangent space from Section~\ref{sec:notation}: the neighborhood PCA basis provides a local linear model of the representation manifold around $\mathbf{x}_i$.

\subsection{Tangent-constrained update}

Given the ambient erasure direction $\mathbf{u}_i$ from Eq.~\ref{eq:ambient_direction}, \TanGCE{} defines the tangent-constrained direction by projecting onto the local tangent space:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_direction}
\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}} = \mathbf{P}_{T_i} \, \mathbf{u}_i = \mathbf{B}_i (\mathbf{B}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i).
\end{equation}
The projected direction is \emph{not} re-normalized to unit length. Its norm $\|\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}\| \leq 1$ acts as a confidence weight: when the ambient concept gradient is mostly normal to the manifold, $\|\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}\| \ll 1$, and the edit is naturally attenuated.

\TanGCE{} then applies the update
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_erase}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \mathbf{x}_i - \lambda \, (\mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}) \, \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}.
\end{equation}
The effective step size is $\lambda \|\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}\|^2$, which is maximized when the gradient lies entirely in the tangent space and vanishes when it is entirely off-manifold.

This construction is the core difference between \TanGCE{} and its ambient counterpart. \AmbGCE{} applies the update in the full hidden space, whereas \TanGCE{} restricts the update to tangent directions supported by the local geometry of the representations.

\subsection{Why tangent projection permits larger $\lambda$}

\AmbGCE{} edits $\mathbf{x}_i$ along $\mathbf{u}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, which can contain a large normal component relative to $T_{\mathbf{x}_i}\mathcal{M}$. If $\mathbf{u}_i^{\perp}$ is large, increasing $\lambda$ pushes the representation away from the local manifold approximation and can distort unrelated structure or preserve recoverable signal. \TanGCE{} removes this normal component at first order by replacing $\mathbf{u}_i$ with $\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}$. This geometric mechanism can permit operation at larger erasure strengths than ambient updates.

\subsection{Iterative \TanGCE{} and relation to baselines}

We implement \TanGCE{} using an iterative protocol with probe recycling. Each round begins by training a fresh concept scorer on the current representations, computing per-sample gradients, and applying the tangent-constrained update in Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase}. This turns the method into an iterative nonlinear eraser: every new scorer is trained specifically to recover whatever target information survived the previous round. The tangent estimator is also refit in every round to track the evolving local geometry.

The rationale follows Elazar and Goldberg \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}: success against a single trained classifier is not evidence that the concept has been exhausted. We therefore evaluate every erasure method against a retrained probe after each full erasure procedure, and the erasure itself iterates to remove residual recoverable signal.

This setup also clarifies the relation between \TanGCE{} and the baselines. IGBP \citep{iskander-etal-2023-shielded} is the closest prior nonlinear baseline, performing a one-shot correction to the current decision boundary in each round. \AmbGCE{} is the direct ablation of \TanGCE{}: it keeps the same fresh-scorer loop but applies the update in the full ambient space. \TanGCE{} is the full method, adding the tangent-space restriction.

Two diagnostics verify tangent quality throughout the iterative process. \emph{Tangent Gradient Capture} (TGC) measures the fraction of the leakage gradient lying within the estimated tangent space. \emph{Tangent Neighbor Residual} (TNR) measures the fraction of local secant variation not explained by the tangent basis. High TGC and low TNR indicate that the local linear approximation is sufficiently faithful for the tangent-constrained update to be meaningful.
