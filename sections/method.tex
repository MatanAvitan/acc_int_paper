\section{Method: \TanGCE{}}
\label{sec:method}

We now introduce \emph{Tangent Gradient-based Concept Erasure} (\TanGCE{}), the method presented in this paper. The full pipeline has two phases. \textbf{Phase~1} is two closed-form, one-shot moment-matching projections that exhaust all first- and second-moment concept signal in the representation. \textbf{Phase~2} is the iterative tangent-constrained loop that erases the residual nonlinear concept structure left after Phase~1. The starting point of Phase~2 is a local manifold approximation of representation space: if the neighborhood of a representation $\mathbf{x}$ is well approximated by a low-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$, then the relevant first-order directions are those in the tangent space $T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M}$ rather than arbitrary ambient directions in $\mathbb{R}^d$. We do not observe this tangent space directly. Instead, we approximate it with local PCA/SVD on neighborhood secants and use that first-order estimate to constrain the erasure update. Each iteration of Phase~2 therefore follows the same four-step pattern: train a fresh concept scorer, estimate a local tangent basis, project the scorer gradient into that tangent space, and apply the resulting constrained update.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Overview of \TanGCE{}. Phase~1 applies two closed-form, one-shot moment-matching projections (LEACE then rank-2 covariance projection) on full training data. Phase~2 iterates a fresh-scorer / local-PCA / $\sigma$-preconditioned trust-region update on the residual.}
\label{alg:tangce}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Representations $\mathbf{X} = \{\mathbf{x}_i\}_{i=1}^N$, concept labels $\mathbf{y}$, trust-region scale $\varepsilon$ (default $0.1$), anisotropy exponent $\alpha$ (default $\tfrac{1}{2}$), neighborhood size $k$, retained-variance rule for tangent rank, scorer hidden size $h$ (default $512$), number of rounds $T$.
\STATE \textbf{Phase~1 -- Closed-form moment preprocessing (one-shot, on training data, applied to all splits)}:
\STATE \hspace{1em} (a) Fit LEACE eraser $\mathcal{L}$ on $(\mathbf{X}_{\text{train}}, \mathbf{y})$ and apply: $\mathbf{X} \leftarrow \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{X})$. \hfill (rank-1 mean-shift)
\STATE \hspace{1em} (b) Compute $\Delta\mathbf{\Sigma} = \mathbf{\Sigma}_{+} - \mathbf{\Sigma}_{-}$ on the LEACE-projected $\mathbf{X}_{\text{train}}$, take the top-$2$ eigenvectors of $\Delta\mathbf{\Sigma}$ by $|\lambda|$, orthonormalize $\mathbf{D} = \text{QR}([\hat{\mathbf{d}}_{\text{mean}}\,|\,\text{eigvecs}])$, and project: $\mathbf{X} \leftarrow \mathbf{X} - \mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}^\top \mathbf{X}$. \hfill (rank-2 covariance asymmetry)
\STATE \textbf{Phase~2 -- Iterative tangent-constrained refinement}:
\FOR{$t = 1, \ldots, T$}
    \STATE Train a fresh nonlinear concept scorer $f_t$ (2-layer MLP, hidden $h$) on the current representations.
    \FOR{each sample $\mathbf{x}_i$}
        \STATE Retrieve the $k$ nearest neighbors of $\mathbf{x}_i$ and form local secant vectors.
        \STATE Run local PCA on those secants to obtain a tangent basis $\mathbf{B}_i$, projector $\mathbf{P}_{T_i} = \mathbf{B}_i \mathbf{B}_i^\top$, and singular values $\boldsymbol\sigma_i$.
        \STATE Compute the ambient unit gradient direction $\mathbf{u}_i = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_t(\mathbf{x}_i) / \|\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)\|$.
        \STATE Project it onto the tangent space: $\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}} = \mathbf{P}_{T_i} \mathbf{u}_i$, and form the unit-step displacement $\mathbf{v}_i = (\mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}) \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}$.
        \STATE Compute the local FMaHa scale $s_i = R_i^{\mathrm{white}} \cdot s_i^{\mathrm{maha}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:s_fmaha}) and the per-sample trust-region step $\lambda_i = \min(\lambda_{\max}, \varepsilon s_i / \|\mathbf{C}_i^{\,\alpha}\mathbf{v}_i\|)$.
        \STATE Apply the $\sigma$-preconditioned tangent update (Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase_cov}):
        \STATE \hspace{2em}$\displaystyle \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{x}_i - \lambda_i \, \mathbf{C}_i^{\,\alpha} \, \mathbf{v}_i.$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Replace $\mathbf{X}$ by the updated representations $\tilde{\mathbf{X}}$ and continue to the next round.
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} intervened representations after $T$ fresh-scorer rounds.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Phase~1: Closed-form moment preprocessing}
\label{sec:moment_preproc}

Before iteration begins, we apply two closed-form, one-shot moment-matching projections that exhaust all linear (1st-moment) and rank-2 quadratic (2nd-moment) concept signal in the representation. The iterative tangent loop then operates on the residual nonlinear structure that no closed-form linear projection can reach.

\paragraph{LEACE (1st moment).}
We fit a LEACE eraser \citep{belrose-etal-2023-leace} on $(\mathbf{X}_{\text{train}}, \mathbf{y})$ and apply it to all three splits. LEACE removes the rank-1 cross-covariance $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{xz}$: after this step, no \emph{linear} classifier can recover the concept above majority-class accuracy on training data, with provably minimum representation damage among affine projections enforcing this invariant. In our setting LEACE removes one rank from a representation of dimension $d \in \{2048, 3072, 3584\}$ at negligible utility cost.

\paragraph{Covariance-asymmetry projection (2nd moment).}
LEACE matches class means but leaves the within-class covariance asymmetry
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_sigma}
\Delta\mathbf{\Sigma} \;=\; \mathbf{\Sigma}_{+} - \mathbf{\Sigma}_{-}
\end{equation}
unchanged, where $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{\pm}$ are the per-class within-class covariances of the LEACE-projected features. A 2-layer MLP probe can still recover concept information from variance asymmetry along axes where $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{+}$ and $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{-}$ differ, even when the class means are equal — squared features expose this signal that is invisible to any linear classifier. We therefore append a rank-$2$ projection: take the top-$2$ eigenvectors of $\Delta\mathbf{\Sigma}$ ranked by $|\lambda|$, orthonormalize with a residual mean direction via QR,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:covmatch_basis}
\mathbf{D} \;=\; \mathrm{QR}\!\left([\,\hat{\mathbf{d}}_{\text{mean}} \;\big|\; \mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2\,]\right) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{d \times 3},
\end{equation}
and project this rank-$3$ subspace out of every split:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:covmatch_apply}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \;=\; \mathbf{x} \;-\; \mathbf{D}\mathbf{D}^\top \mathbf{x}.
\end{equation}
Because LEACE has already aligned the class means, $\hat{\mathbf{d}}_{\text{mean}} \approx \mathbf{0}$ and the QR step keeps only the two eigenvector directions in practice, so the effective preprocessing rank removed is two. This step is a special case of the $k$-LEACE-style higher-moment erasure framework of \citet{singh-ravfogel-2024-representation-surgery}; we use the rank-2 instance because second-order eigenvectors are stable to estimate from $n \approx 7000$ training examples while higher-order tensors are not.

\paragraph{Why two stages, both closed-form.}
Each stage removes a structurally distinct part of the concept signal that the other cannot reach: LEACE eliminates the mean-shift any linear classifier exploits; the covariance projection eliminates the variance asymmetry a quadratic classifier exploits. Both stages have closed-form solutions on full training data, making them more accurate than per-step or kernelized estimates, and each costs $\mathcal{O}(d^2)$ time and a single rank-1-or-2 projection at inference. They remove rank $\leq 3$ dimensions out of $d \in \{2048, 3072, 3584\}$ — a negligible fraction of the representation budget — while exposing the residual nonlinear concept structure that the iterative loop in Phase~2 is designed to erase.

\subsection{Local tangent estimation}

For each query point $\mathbf{x}_i$, we estimate the tangent space $T_{\mathbf{x}_i}\mathcal{M}$ via local PCA \ye{so far you wrote PCA/SVD} on its $k$-nearest neighbors. Let $\mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x}_i) = \{\mathbf{x}_{j_1}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_{j_k}\}$ denote the $k$ nearest neighbors of $\mathbf{x}_i$ in the training set \ye{including i?}. We form secant vectors $\mathbf{s}_{j_m} = \mathbf{x}_{j_m} - \mathbf{x}_i$ and compute the local covariance:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:local_cov}
\mathbf{C}_i = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{m=1}^{k} \mathbf{s}_{j_m} \mathbf{s}_{j_m}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}.
\end{equation}
The top $r$ eigenvectors of $\mathbf{C}_i$ form the tangent basis
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_basis}
\mathbf{B}_i = [\mathbf{b}_i^{(1)}, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_i^{(r)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r},
\end{equation}
where the intrinsic dimension $r$ is selected automatically by a retained-variance rule. The resulting orthogonal projector onto the estimated tangent space is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_proj}
\mathbf{P}_{T_i} = \mathbf{B}_i \mathbf{B}_i^\top.
\end{equation}

This is the operational approximation used in \TanGCE{}: the neighborhood PCA basis serves as an empirical local linear basis. We treat it as a practical first-order stand-in for a tangent space, not as a theorem-level guarantee that the representations form a smooth manifold.

\subsection{Tangent-constrained update}

To define the tangent-constrained update, we start from the ambient unit gradient direction
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ambient_direction}
\mathbf{u}_i = \frac{\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i)}{\|\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i)\|}.
\end{equation}
\TanGCE{} then defines the tangent-constrained direction by projecting onto the local tangent space:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_direction}
\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}} = \mathbf{P}_{T_i} \, \mathbf{u}_i = \mathbf{B}_i (\mathbf{B}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i).
\end{equation}
The projected direction is \emph{not} re-normalized to unit length. Its norm $\|\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}\| \leq 1$ acts as an overlap weight: when the ambient concept gradient has little support in the estimated tangent space, the update is naturally attenuated.

\TanGCE{} then applies the update
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_erase}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \mathbf{x}_i - \lambda \, (\mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}) \, \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}.
\end{equation}
\ye{lambda wasn't defined yet afaik}
The update in Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase} is a heuristic design choice, not the solution to an explicit optimization problem. It projects the representation onto the estimated tangent direction and subtracts a scaled multiple, analogous to a one-step descent along the constrained direction.

The effective step size is $\lambda \|\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}\|^2$. It is larger when the gradient has more support in the estimated tangent space and smaller when it has less, but this does not guarantee monotonic improvement in either leakage or utility.

This construction is the core difference between \TanGCE{} and its ambient counterpart. \AmbGCE{} applies the update in the full hidden space, whereas \TanGCE{} restricts the update to directions supported by the local geometry estimated in the model's native coordinate system. We do not claim invariance under coordinate changes; the tangent basis is computed in the LayerNorm-induced geometry of the given representations.

Equation~\ref{eq:tangent_erase} treats every direction within the tangent space symmetrically. We retain it here as the conceptual entry point and as the $\alpha = 0$ ablation, but the canonical \TanGCE{} update used throughout the paper is the $\sigma$-preconditioned generalization derived in §\ref{sec:cov_tangce} (Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase_cov}), which respects the anisotropy of the local manifold by reweighting each tangent direction with its associated singular value.

\subsection{Why tangent projection can help at larger \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda}}

\AmbGCE{} edits $\mathbf{x}_i$ in the full ambient space, where the gradient may contain components that are poorly aligned with the local representation support. Under the local manifold view, those components are the ones most likely to move the representation away from the neighborhood geometry captured by natural data. \TanGCE{} removes them at first order by replacing $\mathbf{u}_i$ with its projection $\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}$ into the estimated tangent space. This motivates the hypothesis that tangent-constrained updates can remain effective at larger step sizes. We treat that statement as empirical rather than guaranteed, and evaluate it directly in the results section.

\subsection{Adaptive per-sample step size: closed-form \texorpdfstring{$\lambda_i$}{lambda}}
\label{sec:closed_form_lambda}

A single global $\lambda$ over-edits some samples while under-editing others. Confident samples (large gradient norm and well-aligned tangent) move proportionally more for any fixed scalar; uncertain samples (small or off-manifold gradient) are dragged by the same scalar in noise-dominated directions. We address this with a per-sample step size $\lambda_i$ that respects the \emph{local trust region} of the manifold approximation.

\paragraph{Trust region.} Recall that $\mathbf{B}_i$ is the local PCA basis at $\mathbf{x}_i$ and let $s_i$ be a per-sample scale on which the linear tangent approximation is reliable. We require that no sample moves further than a fraction $\varepsilon$ of this local scale:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:trust_region}
\| \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i - \mathbf{x}_i \|_2 \;\leq\; \varepsilon \cdot s_i.
\end{equation}
Here $\varepsilon \in (0, \infty)$ is a single global trust-region scale, dimensionless and shared across panels. Smaller $\varepsilon$ yields surgical edits inside each local manifold patch; larger $\varepsilon$ admits steps that may leave the patch. We use $\varepsilon = 0.1$ throughout (an order of magnitude smaller than the canonical $\varepsilon = 0.3$ used in our earlier ablations); see §~\ref{sec:results} for the empirical effect of this choice on tight-budget surgicality. The simplest choice is $s_i = r_i$, the mean distance to the $k$ nearest neighbors — the radial neighborhood scale. We use a tighter, anisotropy-aware scale by default; see Eq.~\ref{eq:s_fmaha} below.

\paragraph{Closed-form solution.} Substituting Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase} into Eq.~\ref{eq:trust_region}, the constraint becomes
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lambda_constraint}
\lambda_i \cdot |\mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}| \cdot \|\mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}\| \;\leq\; \varepsilon \cdot s_i.
\end{equation}
Let $\mathbf{v}_i = (\mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}) \, \mathbf{u}_i^{\mathrm{tan}}$ be the per-sample edit displacement at $\lambda{=}1$. The largest $\lambda_i$ satisfying Eq.~\ref{eq:lambda_constraint} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lambda_closed_form}
\boxed{ \;\;
\lambda_i \;=\; \min\!\left( \lambda_{\max}, \; \frac{\varepsilon \cdot s_i}{\|\mathbf{v}_i\|} \right) \;\;
}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{\max}$ is a safety cap that prevents runaway updates when $\|\mathbf{v}_i\| \to 0$ (gradient nearly orthogonal to the tangent space). In practice we set $\lambda_{\max}=64$, large enough that it binds only for samples whose gradient has near-zero tangent component.

\paragraph{Anisotropy-aware local scale.} Setting $s_i = r_i$ (mean $k$-NN distance) is the simplest valid choice, but treats every direction equally. The local manifold is generally an \emph{ellipsoid}, not a sphere: along the dominant principal directions of the local PCA, neighbors extend much further than along thin (low-$\sigma$) directions. We therefore prefer the \emph{whitened-tangent neighborhood radius}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:s_fmaha}
s_i \;=\; R_i^{\mathrm{white}} \cdot s_i^{\mathrm{maha}}, \qquad
R_i^{\mathrm{white}} = \mathrm{median}_{j \in \mathcal{N}_k(i)} \, \|\mathbf{B}_i^\top (\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i) \,/\, \boldsymbol\sigma_i \|_2,
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol\sigma_i = (\sigma_{i,1}, \ldots, \sigma_{i,r})$ is the local PCA spectrum and division is component-wise. The factor $R_i^{\mathrm{white}}$ measures how far neighbors typically reach in standardized tangent coordinates; the factor $s_i^{\mathrm{maha}} = 1 / \|\boldsymbol\alpha_i / (\boldsymbol\sigma_i + \delta)\|_2$ rescales the per-sample step direction by its harmonic-$\sigma$ tangent length, where $\boldsymbol\alpha_i$ is the unit step direction in tangent coordinates. We refer to this scaling as \emph{fractional Mahalanobis} (FMaHa): under it, $\varepsilon$ is interpretable as a fraction of the local whitened-tangent neighborhood. Setting $s_i = r_i$ recovers the original Euclidean radial scale and is reported as an ablation.

\paragraph{Why this matters.} Eq.~\ref{eq:lambda_closed_form} is a genuine \emph{trust region} per sample: confident samples move by exactly $\varepsilon \cdot r_i$ along their tangent; uncertain samples (small $\|\mathbf{v}_i\|$) get a larger nominal $\lambda_i$ but the actual movement $\lambda_i \cdot \|\mathbf{v}_i\|$ remains bounded by $\varepsilon \cdot r_i$ (or by the safety cap, in which case movement is small because $\|\mathbf{v}_i\|$ is small). This per-sample adaptation removes a hyperparameter (one global $\lambda$) and replaces it with a single dimensionless surgicality knob $\varepsilon$.

The closed-form expression in Eq.~\ref{eq:lambda_closed_form} requires no bisection or auxiliary optimization: each iteration computes $\mathbf{v}_i$ once via a forward pass through the scorer, $s_i$ once via the cached $k$-NN distances and the local SVD spectrum, and $\lambda_i$ in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ per row. This makes the per-sample trust region essentially free relative to the cost of refitting the scorer and tangent.

\subsection{Anisotropic update via the local \texorpdfstring{$\sigma$}{sigma}-spectrum}
\label{sec:cov_tangce}

The closed-form $\lambda_i$ above controls the \emph{magnitude} of each sample's step. Its \emph{direction} is still isotropic in the tangent basis: every tangent component of $\mathbf{v}_i$ is rescaled by the same scalar $\lambda_i$. But the local PCA already tells us that the manifold is anisotropic — eigenvalues $\sigma_{i,k}^2$ at sample $i$ differ by orders of magnitude across $k$. Treating all tangent directions as equally safe to move along is exactly the assumption that the neighborhood is a sphere; in practice it is much closer to an ellipsoid stretched along the dominant principal directions and pinched in the rank-truncation tail. An isotropic step over-edits thin directions where the linear approximation is most fragile, and under-edits fat directions where the manifold easily accommodates further displacement.

\paragraph{$\sigma$-eigenbasis decomposition.} Decompose the per-sample tangent displacement $\mathbf{v}_i$ in the local PCA basis:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eigen_decomp}
\mathbf{v}_i \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{r} a_{i,k} \, \mathbf{b}_i^{(k)}, \qquad a_{i,k} = \langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{b}_i^{(k)} \rangle,
\end{equation}
where $\{\mathbf{b}_i^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{r}$ are the columns of $\mathbf{B}_i$ (eigenvectors of the local secant covariance, Eq.~\ref{eq:local_cov}) with associated singular values $\sigma_{i,k}$. The local covariance can be written as $\mathbf{C}_i = \mathbf{B}_i \, \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_{i,1}^2, \ldots, \sigma_{i,r}^2) \, \mathbf{B}_i^\top$.

\paragraph{Anisotropic update.} We replace the isotropic step in Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase} by a covariance-preconditioned step that respects the local ellipsoid. Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_erase_cov}
\boxed{ \;\;
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i \;=\; \mathbf{x}_i - \lambda_i \, \mathbf{C}_i^{\,\alpha} \, \mathbf{v}_i \;=\; \mathbf{x}_i - \lambda_i \sum_{k=1}^{r} \sigma_{i,k}^{\,2\alpha} \, a_{i,k} \, \mathbf{b}_i^{(k)} \;\;
}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \geq 0$ is an anisotropy exponent. The effect is direct: each tangent coefficient $a_{i,k}$ is reweighted by $\sigma_{i,k}^{2\alpha}$, so the update moves more along high-variance principal directions (where the manifold supports larger displacements) and less along thin directions (where the linear approximation is fragile).

\paragraph{Three regimes.} Three values of $\alpha$ have natural interpretations:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$\alpha = 0$}: $\mathbf{C}_i^{\,0} = \mathbf{I}$, the update reduces to Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase}. Reported as an ablation.
\item \textbf{$\alpha = \tfrac{1}{2}$}: a \emph{mild} anisotropy. Each direction is reweighted by $\sigma_{i,k}$ rather than by $\sigma_{i,k}^2$, preserving information from low-variance directions while still respecting the dominant geometry. \textbf{This is the default in this paper.}
\item \textbf{$\alpha = 1$}: full Mahalanobis preconditioning. The update direction $\mathbf{C}_i \mathbf{v}_i$ is the first-order optimum of $\min_{\boldsymbol\delta} \langle \mathbf{g}_i, \boldsymbol\delta \rangle$ subject to the Mahalanobis trust region $\boldsymbol\delta^\top \mathbf{C}_i^{-1} \boldsymbol\delta \leq \varepsilon^2$. Reported as an ablation; risks suppressing low-variance signal that may carry residual concept information.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Trust region preserved.} The trust-region magnitude in Eq.~\ref{eq:lambda_closed_form} is enforced after preconditioning, against the transformed step $\mathbf{C}_i^{\,\alpha} \mathbf{v}_i$ (i.e.\ we substitute $\|\mathbf{C}_i^{\,\alpha} \mathbf{v}_i\|$ for $\|\mathbf{v}_i\|$ in the denominator). The actual displacement $\| \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i - \mathbf{x}_i \|_2$ therefore remains bounded by $\varepsilon \cdot s_i$, exactly as in the isotropic case. Anisotropy reshapes the \emph{direction} of the step, not its budget.

\paragraph{Implementation.} Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase_cov} reuses the SVD already computed for the tangent basis; the per-sample spectrum $(\sigma_{i,k})_k$ is cached during local PCA. Concretely, the step decomposition (Eq.~\ref{eq:eigen_decomp}), eigenvalue reweighting, and reprojection to the ambient space are three batched einsums over the $(N, d, r)$ basis tensor. No additional SVDs and no per-sample loops are required. \TanGCE{} therefore inherits the same per-iteration cost as the isotropic update, modulo a single extra $\mathcal{O}(N d r)$ matrix multiplication.

\subsection{Iterative \TanGCE{} and relation to baselines}

We implement \TanGCE{} using an iterative protocol with probe recycling. Each round begins by training a fresh concept scorer on the current representations, computing per-sample gradients, and applying the tangent-constrained update in Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase}. This turns the method into an iterative nonlinear eraser: every new scorer is trained specifically to recover whatever target information survived the previous round. The tangent estimator is also refit in every round to track the evolving local geometry.

The rationale follows Elazar and Goldberg \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}: success against a single trained classifier is not evidence that the concept has been exhausted. We therefore evaluate every erasure method against a retrained probe after each full erasure procedure, and the erasure itself iterates to remove residual recoverable signal. This nonlinear fresh-scorer loop is the conceptual analogue of iterative linear removal in INLP \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null}, but with a newly trained nonlinear scorer at each round.

This setup also clarifies the relation between \TanGCE{} and the baselines. IGBP \citep{iskander-etal-2023-shielded} is the closest prior nonlinear baseline, performing a one-shot correction to the current decision boundary in each round. \AmbGCE{} is the direct ablation of \TanGCE{}: it keeps the same fresh-scorer loop but applies the update in the full ambient space. \TanGCE{} is the full method, adding the tangent-space restriction.

Two diagnostics monitor tangent quality throughout the iterative process. \emph{Tangent Gradient Capture} (TGC) measures the fraction of the leakage gradient lying within the estimated tangent space. \emph{Tangent Neighbor Residual} (TNR) measures the fraction of local secant variation not explained by the tangent basis. High TGC and low TNR indicate that the local linear approximation is informative enough for the tangent-constrained update to be meaningful.
