\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

This draft separates two evidence tiers. Main-text quantitative claims use only the paper runs that were complete when the manuscript snapshot was assembled: the sycophancy tangent-vs-ambient sweep at layers 4 and 8, the Bias in Bios tangent-vs-ambient sweep at layers 4 and 8, the layer-8 iterative safety sweep, and the completed geometry summaries for gender and sycophancy. Broader repository snapshot tables from completed reports are included in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_snapshots} for status tracking, but they are not promoted to the main claim set below.

\subsection{Sycophancy: Nonlinear Concept Erasure}
Table~\ref{tab:completed_results_summary} shows the layer-8 comparison at $\lambda=1.0$. Tangent erasure yields substantially lower leakage (0.105 vs. 0.231), consistent with results across all completed layer/lambda pairs: at layer 4, the best tangent point reaches 0.127 leakage versus 0.168 for ambient.

The lower leakage comes at a cost. Tangent produces larger displacement (0.728 vs. 0.048) and sharper utility loss (-20.4 vs. -17.0 percentage points), patterns that hold across the completed sweep. The evidence therefore supports better concept removal but does not yet establish a frontier advantage in leakage-utility-displacement tradeoffs.

\subsection{Safety: Surgicality and Helpfulness}
The completed iterative safety sweep at layer 8 shows tangent erasure reducing leakage far more aggressively than ambient (Table~\ref{tab:completed_results_summary}). At $\lambda=1.0$, leakage is 0.068 for tangent versus 0.252 for ambient.

Again, lower leakage requires higher cost. Tangent produces larger displacement (2.68 vs. 0.20) and a steeper helpfulness penalty (-0.060 vs. -0.021), indicating a utility-surgicality tradeoff rather than unambiguous improvement.

\subsection{Bias in Bios: Diagnostic Baseline}
Table~\ref{tab:completed_results_summary} shows that tangent reaches substantially lower gender leakage than ambient at layer 8, $\lambda=1.0$ (0.062 vs. 0.221), mirroring layer-4 results (0.084 vs. 0.213 at best points).

This stronger removal comes with utility and displacement costs. At layer 8, tangent reduces control-task accuracy (0.513 vs. 0.536) and requires larger displacement (0.847 vs. 0.156). The linear anchor therefore supports stronger removal, not yet a cleaner utility-preserving regime.

The same layer-8 Qwen snapshot also provides a descriptive comparison against the implemented linear baselines. On Bias in Bios, both linear projection and INLP remain near 0.198 leakage with profession accuracy around 0.539, whereas tangent reaches 0.062 leakage with profession accuracy 0.513. On sycophancy, linear projection and INLP both land at 0.163 leakage with answer-matching deltas around -16.6 percentage points, whereas tangent reaches 0.071 leakage with a -18.0 point answer-matching delta. We treat these cross-family numbers as descriptive rather than as matched-frontier claims, because the evaluation protocol intentionally uses nonlinear probes for nonlinear erasers and linear probes for linear erasers.

\subsection{Geometric Evidence and Off-Manifold Displacement}
\input{tables/geometry_contrast_summary}

Table~\ref{tab:geometry_contrast_summary} demonstrates that sycophancy is much more nonlinear than gender: its intrinsic dimension (34--53 across layers 4, 8, 12) is far lower than gender's (365--461), and its local-vs-global angle is much larger (76.9--82.6$^\circ$ vs. 51.8--55.8$^\circ$). Pairwise scorer agreement remains high in the completed runs, suggesting coherence in the local direction field despite weak global summary.

This geometry contrast validates sycophancy as the primary case for manifold-aware erasure methods. However, the completed paper runs do not yet prove that this geometric advantage translates into lower-displacement or higher-utility erasure points on any frontier.

\paragraph{Appendix-Supporting Evidence.}
Detailed geometric analyses supporting these claims are provided in the Appendix, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Subspace Sufficiency (Q1):} Evidence that task-specific computation is concentrated in a low-dimensional linear subspace.
    \item \textbf{Perturbation Sensitivity (Q2):} Comparison of perturbations in high-variance and orthogonal subspaces, which supports low-dimensional sensitivity concentration but does not by itself prove curved-manifold causality.
    \item \textbf{Representation Connectivity (Q3):} Analysis of the connected, approximately convex topology of the representation manifold.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Layer Accumulation:} Results on how projection error accumulates across the network.
\end{itemize}
These appendix-scale geometry analyses remain important support for the mechanism story, but the main-text quantitative claims should currently be limited to the completed paper runs summarized above.

\paragraph{Broader Repository Snapshot.}
The broader iterative reports recorded in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_snapshots} are directionally consistent with the main-text picture. In both the sycophancy and safety repository snapshots (Tables~\ref{tab:repo_snapshot_sycophancy} and \ref{tab:repo_snapshot_safety}), deeper-layer iterative tangent runs continue to reduce leakage more than ambient baselines. We include those tables to document the current state of the project and to motivate the next matched-leakage analyses, but we do not rely on them for the central paper claims.
