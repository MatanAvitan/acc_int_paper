\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate seven erasure methods across three concept domains---sycophancy, gender (Bias in Bios), and safety---at layer~8 of Qwen2.5-0.5B. Each method's erasure strength $\lambda^*$ is selected from the same shared validation protocol described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Our main metrics are erasure precision, measured by retrained probe accuracy on the target concept, and surgicality, measured by change in control-task performance.

\input{tables/full_comparison}

\subsection{\TanGCE{} Achieves the Best Erasure Precision Across All Domains}

Table~\ref{tab:full_comparison} shows that \TanGCE{} achieves the lowest nonlinear leakage across all three domains: 0.602 on sycophancy (vs.\ 0.681 for LEACE, 0.703 for \AmbGCE{}), 0.618 on gender (vs.\ 0.701 for \AmbGCE{}), and 0.663 on safety (vs.\ 0.778 for \AmbGCE{}). Linear methods (Linear Projection, INLP) barely reduce leakage below random baselines, confirming that concept information is predominantly encoded nonlinearly.

\subsection{\TanGCE{} Remains Surgical on Control Concepts}

Table~\ref{tab:full_comparison} reports $\Delta$Ctrl, the change in downstream control task accuracy relative to clean representations. For sycophancy (answer matching), \TanGCE{} incurs a small $-1.5$pp drop; all other methods preserve perfect accuracy. For gender (profession classification), \TanGCE{} drops only $-0.8$pp (0.531 vs.\ 0.539 clean). On safety, \TanGCE{} \emph{improves} helpfulness ($+3.9$pp), suggesting that removing safety-concept directions frees capacity for the helpfulness task.

Table~\ref{tab:lambda_sweep} isolates the effect of step size by running only \AmbGCE{} and \TanGCE{} at three fixed $\lambda$ values ($0.5, 4.0, 8.0$) on the same three domains as above (Qwen2.5-0.5B, layer~8, same train/val/test splits and fresh-probe evaluation protocol as in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}). As $\lambda$ increases, \AmbGCE{} becomes unstable: its nonlinear leakage worsens sharply at high step sizes, rising to 0.734 on sycophancy, 0.702 on gender, and 0.770 on safety at $\lambda{=}8$. \TanGCE{} behaves in the opposite way, improving as $\lambda$ increases and reaching its best precision at the largest tested step size in all three domains.

This pattern is consistent with the manifold hypothesis motivating the method. If the ambient gradient has a substantial off-manifold component, then increasing $\lambda$ does not simply erase more target information; it also pushes the representation into regions where unrelated structure is distorted. Projecting onto the local tangent approximation suppresses those off-manifold components, which is why \TanGCE{} can safely use larger erasure strengths.

\subsection{Geometric Characterization: Low-Rank Subspace Isolation and Manifold Curvature}
\label{sec:geometry}

The preceding results establish that \TanGCE{} achieves stronger nonlinear erasure than \AmbGCE{}. This subsection provides the geometric evidence behind that advantage, organized around two distinct observations that operate at different levels of the geometry. The first is a \emph{global linear} property: each domain's representations concentrate near a low-dimensional PCA subspace, and sycophancy's subspace is geometrically isolated from those of other tasks. The second is a \emph{local nonlinear} property: sycophancy's subspace is curved, meaning the locally estimated tangent direction at each sample differs substantially from the globally fitted PCA direction. The first observation explains why targeted erasure is feasible and why ambient-space edits risk spurious interference with unrelated tasks. The second explains why global linear methods (LEACE, Linear Projection) are insufficient for sycophancy and why a locally-estimated tangent constraint is specifically needed.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We run two diagnostic experiments on layer~8 representations of Qwen2.5-0.5B across five domains: Bias in Bios (gender), code generation, mathematical reasoning, content safety, and sycophancy. For each domain we collect 500 labeled representations and estimate the global intrinsic dimension $m$ via a retained-variance rule applied to a held-out reference set of 2{,}000 samples (not used in erasure training). All geometry experiments use the same evaluation split as the main comparison.

\paragraph{Q1: Cross-domain subspace isolation.}
For each ordered pair of domains $(\mathrm{src}, \mathrm{tgt})$, we fit PCA on \emph{target}-domain representations to dimension $k \approx m_{\mathrm{tgt}}$, project \emph{source}-domain representations onto the resulting linear subspace, and measure top-1 next-token agreement and KL divergence relative to the unmodified source representations. High agreement indicates that source representations lie within the target's principal subspace; collapse indicates near-orthogonality of the two subspaces.

Figure~\ref{fig:geom_combined} (left) shows the $5 \times 5$ transfer matrix. The diagonal is uniformly high (top-1 $\geq 0.88$), confirming that each domain's principal subspace captures the directions that matter for its own predictions. Off the diagonal, sycophancy is the structural outlier: projecting sycophancy representations onto the biasbios subspace yields top-1 agreement of $0.002$ ($\mathrm{KL} = 7.4$), and onto the code subspace gives $0.13$ ($\mathrm{KL} = 6.6$). No other pair approaches this degree of isolation. Conversely, math and safety share substantial subspace overlap (mutual top-1 $\approx 0.88$--$0.92$), consistent with those tasks drawing on shared general-language structure. The pattern is stable across layers~4, 8, and 16 (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_snapshots}).

This isolation has a direct implication for ambient-space erasure. An edit applied in the full hidden space will have nonzero projections onto the biasbios and code subspaces---directions that carry essentially no sycophancy signal. \TanGCE{} attenuates those components by restricting the update to the locally estimated subspace; \AmbGCE{} applies the full ambient gradient, including its projections onto these geometrically irrelevant directions.

\paragraph{Q2: Subspace sensitivity concentration.}
To verify that the identified PCA subspaces are causally load-bearing and not merely high-variance summaries, we perturb each sample by $\epsilon \|\mathbf{x}\|$ in three directions: a random direction within the principal subspace (on-subspace), a random direction orthogonal to it (off-subspace), and a random ambient direction. A ratio $\mathrm{off\text{-}top1} / \mathrm{on\text{-}top1} > 1$ indicates that on-subspace perturbations are more disruptive than equal off-subspace perturbations---the subspace concentrates task-relevant computation.

Figure~\ref{fig:geom_combined} (right) shows the off/on ratio at $\epsilon = 0.5$ for all 25 domain pairs. Along the diagonal, all five ratios exceed~1, confirming subspace causal relevance across domains. The effect is sharpest for sycophancy (ratio $= 1.16$): an on-subspace perturbation is $16\%$ more destructive than an equal off-subspace one, the largest asymmetry among the five domains. The off-diagonal cells in the sycophancy column (rows~1--2) show ratios of $0.79$ and $0.72$: the sycophancy subspace is a null direction for biasbios and code predictions, so perturbations within it leave those tasks unaffected while perturbations orthogonal to it do not. Ambient erasure updates that project onto the sycophancy subspace therefore interfere with the orthogonal structure those tasks actually use.

\paragraph{Manifold curvature: why global subspaces are insufficient.}
Q1 and Q2 establish that representations concentrate in a causally relevant low-dimensional linear subspace. A natural response would be to simply remove the global PCA direction of sycophancy---the approach taken by LEACE and Linear Projection. But this strategy implicitly assumes that the subspace is \emph{flat}: that the first global PCA direction provides a reliable approximation to the locally relevant concept direction at any given sample. This assumption can be tested.

\input{tables/geometry_contrast_summary}

Table~\ref{tab:geometry_contrast_summary} measures \emph{manifold curvature} via the local-vs-global angle: the angle between the locally estimated tangent direction at each sample (estimated by neighborhood PCA, exactly as in \TanGCE{}) and the globally fitted first principal component. For a flat linear subspace this angle is $0^\circ$. For a curved manifold it grows as the locally relevant direction rotates across the input space. Sycophancy shows local-vs-global angles of $76.9$--$82.6^\circ$ across layers~4, 8, and 12---nearly orthogonal. Gender shows $51.8$--$55.8^\circ$, indicating a more planar structure. The quantitative implication is immediate: a method that fits a single global concept direction and applies it uniformly is using a direction that is, on average, $\sim 80^\circ$ away from the direction that actually encodes the sycophancy concept at any individual sample. \TanGCE{}'s neighborhood PCA re-estimates this direction locally, recovering the component that globally-fitted methods miss entirely.

This explains the asymmetry in Table~\ref{tab:full_comparison}: LEACE achieves better surgicality on sycophancy because it removes a smaller volume of representation space (one global direction), while \TanGCE{} achieves lower nonlinear leakage because its locally varying direction tracks where the concept actually encodes, even as that direction rotates across the input. The two methods target different levels of the same underlying geometry: LEACE removes the flat global projection; \TanGCE{} follows the curved local manifold.

\paragraph{Summary.}
The two observations connect to different aspects of the \TanGCE{} design. Subspace isolation (Q1/Q2) motivates restricting edits to the directions where sycophancy information actually lives rather than editing the full ambient space. Manifold curvature (Table~\ref{tab:geometry_contrast_summary}) motivates estimating those directions locally at each sample rather than fitting a single global direction. Together they form the geometric case for a method that is both subspace-restricted and locally adaptive---precisely the design of \TanGCE{}.

\subsection{Multi-Model Generalization}
\label{sec:multi_model}

To assess whether \TanGCE{}'s advantage is specific to Qwen2.5-0.5B or holds across model families and scales, we repeat the evaluation at layer~8 across five models spanning three families and parameter counts from 0.5B to 7B: Qwen2.5 (0.5B, 1.5B, 3B), Gemma-2-2B, and Mistral-7B-v0.1. We compare only \TanGCE{} and \AmbGCE{}, the two methods that differ solely in their gradient constraint, isolating the effect of tangent-space projection. The same validation HPO protocol ($\lambda^* \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0\}$, selected by lowest validation nonlinear probe accuracy) is applied uniformly.

\input{tables/multi_model_comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:multi_model} shows that \TanGCE{} achieves lower nonlinear leakage than \AmbGCE{} consistently across all model families and sizes evaluated. The absolute gap narrows somewhat at larger scales but remains in the same direction, suggesting that the tangent projection's benefit is not an artifact of the particular hidden-state geometry of small models. Control accuracy changes ($\Delta$Ctrl) remain small and comparable between the two methods across all settings.

\subsection{Second Vision Benchmark: FairFace}
\label{sec:fairface}

CelebA is a large-scale dataset of celebrity images, which may introduce distribution shifts specific to this demographic slice. To test cross-dataset robustness of our findings in the vision domain, we additionally evaluate on FairFace~\citep{karkkainen2021fairface}, a balanced benchmark designed for diverse real-world face representation across gender, race, and age. We apply all seven erasure methods to CLIP ViT-B/32 \texttt{pooler\_output} embeddings (768-dim) using the same validation HPO protocol as CelebA. For each of the four target concepts (Gender, Race~White, Race~Black, Age~Young), we select 5 low-correlation binary demographic attributes as controls. Erasure strength $\lambda^*$ is selected from $\{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0\}$ by lowest validation nonlinear probe accuracy.

\input{tables/fairface_clip_fair}

Table~\ref{tab:fairface_clip_fair} shows that the pattern from CelebA replicates on FairFace: \TanGCE{} achieves the lowest nonlinear leakage across all four demographic concepts while maintaining comparable control accuracy to \AmbGCE{}. This confirms that the advantage of tangent-constrained erasure is not dataset-specific within the vision domain.
