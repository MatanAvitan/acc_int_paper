\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate seven erasure methods across three concept domains---sycophancy, gender (Bias in Bios), and safety---at layer~8 of Qwen2.5-0.5B. Each method's erasure strength $\lambda^*$ is selected from the same shared validation protocol described in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Our main metrics are erasure precision, measured by retrained probe accuracy on the target concept, and surgicality, measured by change in control-task performance.

\input{tables/full_comparison}

\subsection{\TanGCE{} Achieves the Best Erasure Precision Across All Domains}

Table~\ref{tab:full_comparison} shows that \TanGCE{} achieves the lowest nonlinear leakage across all three domains: 0.602 on sycophancy (vs.\ 0.681 for LEACE, 0.703 for \AmbGCE{}), 0.618 on gender (vs.\ 0.701 for \AmbGCE{}), and 0.663 on safety (vs.\ 0.778 for \AmbGCE{}). Linear methods (Linear Projection, INLP) barely reduce leakage below random baselines, confirming that concept information is predominantly encoded nonlinearly.

\subsection{\TanGCE{} Remains Surgical on Control Concepts}

Table~\ref{tab:full_comparison} reports $\Delta$Ctrl, the change in downstream control task accuracy relative to clean representations. For sycophancy (answer matching), \TanGCE{} incurs a small $-1.5$pp drop; all other methods preserve perfect accuracy. For gender (profession classification), \TanGCE{} drops only $-0.8$pp (0.531 vs.\ 0.539 clean). On safety, \TanGCE{} \emph{improves} helpfulness ($+3.9$pp), suggesting that removing safety-concept directions frees capacity for the helpfulness task.

\subsection{The Lambda Sweep: On-Manifold Updates Support Larger Interventions}
\label{sec:lambda_sweep}

\input{tables/lambda_sweep}

Table~\ref{tab:lambda_sweep} isolates the effect of step size by running only \AmbGCE{} and \TanGCE{} at three fixed $\lambda$ values ($0.5, 4.0, 8.0$) on the same three domains as above (Qwen2.5-0.5B, layer~8, same train/val/test splits and fresh-probe evaluation protocol as in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}). As $\lambda$ increases, \AmbGCE{} becomes unstable: its nonlinear leakage worsens sharply at high step sizes, rising to 0.734 on sycophancy, 0.702 on gender, and 0.770 on safety at $\lambda{=}8$. \TanGCE{} behaves in the opposite way, improving as $\lambda$ increases and reaching its best precision at the largest tested step size in all three domains.

This pattern is consistent with the manifold hypothesis motivating the method. If the ambient gradient has a substantial off-manifold component, then increasing $\lambda$ does not simply erase more target information; it also pushes the representation into regions where unrelated structure is distorted. Projecting onto the local tangent approximation suppresses those off-manifold components, which is why \TanGCE{} can safely use larger erasure strengths.


\subsection{Cross-Modal Generalization: CelebA Visual Attributes}
\label{sec:celeba}

To test whether \TanGCE{} generalizes beyond language model representations, we apply all seven erasure methods to CLIP ViT-B/32 \texttt{pooler\_output} embeddings (768-dim) of CelebA face images (see Section~\ref{sec:experiments} for preprocessing and split details). CelebA provides 40 binary facial attributes, giving a rich surgicality profile: when erasing one attribute, the remaining attributes serve as controls. The same validation-HPO protocol ($\lambda^* \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0\}$ by lowest validation NL accuracy) is applied uniformly across all methods.

We treat this cross-modal block as a generality check rather than the primary evidence block for the paper's central claim.

\subsection{Geometric Characterization: Subspace Isolation and Sensitivity Concentration}
\label{sec:geometry}

The preceding results establish that \TanGCE{} achieves stronger nonlinear erasure than \AmbGCE{}. This subsection provides two empirical measurements that explain \emph{why} tangent projection is particularly effective for sycophancy: the concept's intrinsic subspace is geometrically isolated from other task subspaces, and that isolated subspace is the primary carrier of task-relevant computation.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
We run two diagnostic experiments on layer~8 representations of Qwen2.5-0.5B across five domains: Bias in Bios (gender), code generation, mathematical reasoning, content safety, and sycophancy. For each domain we collect 500 labeled representations and estimate the intrinsic dimension $m$ via a retained-variance rule applied to a held-out reference set of 2{,}000 samples (not used in erasure training). All geometry experiments use the same evaluation split as the main comparison.

\paragraph{Q1: Cross-domain subspace transfer.}
For each ordered pair of domains $(\mathrm{src}, \mathrm{tgt})$, we fit PCA on \emph{target}-domain representations to dimension $k \approx m_{\mathrm{tgt}}$, project \emph{source}-domain representations onto the resulting subspace, and measure top-1 next-token agreement and KL divergence relative to the unmodified source representations. High top-1 agreement indicates that the source domain's representations lie within the target's intrinsic subspace; agreement collapse indicates geometric orthogonality between the two concept subspaces.

Figure~\ref{fig:geom_combined} (left) shows the resulting $5 \times 5$ transfer matrix. The diagonal is uniformly high (top-1 $\geq 0.88$), confirming that each domain's intrinsic subspace captures the directions that matter for its own predictions. Off the diagonal, sycophancy is the structural outlier: projecting sycophancy representations onto the biasbios subspace yields top-1 agreement of $0.002$ ($\mathrm{KL} = 7.4$), and onto the code subspace gives $0.13$ ($\mathrm{KL} = 6.6$). No other pair in the matrix approaches this degree of isolation. Conversely, math and safety share substantial subspace overlap (mutual top-1 $\approx 0.88$--$0.92$), consistent with those tasks drawing on similar general-language structure. The pattern is stable across layers~4, 8, and 16 (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_snapshots}).

This isolation has a direct implication for ambient-space erasure. When the sycophancy concept gradient is applied in the full hidden space, it necessarily has components that project onto the biasbios and code subspaces---directions whose off-diagonal transfer is near zero and that therefore carry no sycophancy signal whatsoever. \TanGCE{} attenuates those components by restricting the update to the local tangent space, which approximates the directions actually used by the representations. \AmbGCE{} applies the full ambient gradient, including its projections onto these geometrically irrelevant directions.

\paragraph{Q2: Perturbation sensitivity.}
To verify that the identified subspaces are causally load-bearing and not merely high-variance summaries, we perturb each sample by $\epsilon \|\mathbf{x}\|$ in three directions: a random direction drawn uniformly within the intrinsic subspace (on-manifold), a random direction orthogonal to it (off-manifold), and a random ambient direction. We then compare top-1 next-token agreement under the three perturbation types. A ratio $\mathrm{off\text{-}top1} / \mathrm{on\text{-}top1} > 1$ indicates that on-subspace perturbations are more disruptive than equal-magnitude off-subspace perturbations---that is, the subspace carries task-relevant computation, not merely low-dimensional structure.

Figure~\ref{fig:geom_combined} (right) shows the off/on top-1 ratio at $\epsilon = 0.5$ for all 25 domain pairs. Along the diagonal---where the source representations and the subspace belong to the same domain---all five ratios exceed~1, confirming that intrinsic subspaces concentrate task-relevant computation. The effect is sharpest for sycophancy (ratio $= 1.16$): an on-manifold perturbation of unit norm is $16\%$ more destructive than an equal off-manifold perturbation, the largest sensitivity asymmetry among the five domains. The effect is consistent at $\epsilon = 1.0$ and $\epsilon = 2.0$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_snapshots}).

The off-diagonal cells reinforce the isolation result from Q1. For biasbios and code representations perturbed within the \emph{sycophancy} subspace (column~5, rows~1--2), the off/on ratio falls to $0.79$ and $0.72$: perturbations within the sycophancy subspace are actually \emph{less} disruptive to biasbios and code predictions than perturbations orthogonal to it. The sycophancy subspace is a null direction for those tasks. This means that ambient erasure updates, which have nonzero projections onto the sycophancy subspace, will inadvertently interfere with the orthogonal structure that biasbios and code actually use.

\paragraph{Geometric summary.}
Taken together, Q1 and Q2 establish three properties of the sycophancy concept at layer~8: its intrinsic subspace is geometrically isolated from other task subspaces (Q1 off-diagonal), it is self-sufficient for sycophancy computation (Q1 diagonal and Q2 diagonal), and it is a null direction for unrelated tasks (Q2 off-diagonal). These are the empirical preconditions for the tangent-constraint design: \TanGCE{} restricts updates to the directions where sycophancy computation lives, while attenuating the ambient components that land in geometrically irrelevant---or actively null---regions of the hidden space.

\input{tables/geometry_contrast_summary}

Table~\ref{tab:geometry_contrast_summary} places these measurements in the context of nonlinearity: sycophancy's intrinsic dimension (34--53 across layers 4, 8, 12) is more than an order of magnitude smaller than gender's (365--461), and its local-vs-global angle (76.9--82.6$^\circ$) indicates that the local tangent direction rotates substantially across the input space---a hallmark of curved concept geometry. The geometry contrast is consistent with sycophancy being the primary case for manifold-aware erasure, while \TanGCE{}'s strong results on gender show that the method generalizes to more linear concepts as well.
