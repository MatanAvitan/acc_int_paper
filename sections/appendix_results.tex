\section{Intuition: ``fraction of the local spectrally-normalized neighborhood''}
\label{sec:appendix_spectral_intuition}

Section~\ref{sec:closed_form_lambda} introduces the per-sample trust region $\| \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i - \mathbf{x}_i \|_2 \leq \varepsilon \cdot s_i$ and offers two choices for the local scale $s_i$: the simple radial $k$-NN distance $r_i$, or the spectrally-normalized tangent radius (Eq.~\ref{eq:s_spectral}). The paper describes the spectral choice as making $\varepsilon$ ``a fraction of the local spectrally-normalized neighborhood.'' This appendix unpacks what that phrase means in concrete terms.

\paragraph{Reading the trust region as a budget.}
With $s_i = r_i$ (the radial choice), the bound reads ``move at most $\varepsilon \cdot$ Euclidean $k$-NN radius.'' This is direction-agnostic: every direction inside ambient space gets the same budget. With $s_i$ defined by Eq.~\ref{eq:s_spectral} (the spectrally-normalized tangent radius), the bound instead reads ``move at most $\varepsilon$ of the neighborhood radius \emph{after dividing each local tangent axis by its own singular value $\sigma_{i,k}$}.'' That distinction is what makes $\varepsilon$ direction-aware. The construction is Mahalanobis covariance normalization expressed through the local SVD: in the tangent basis, $\mathbf{C}_i^{-1/2}$ acts diagonally as $\sigma_{i,k}^{-1}$, so dividing each tangent coordinate by $\sigma_{i,k}$ is the same operation, written in the coordinate system the secant decomposition already gave us.

\paragraph{Worked example.}
Suppose the local tangent space at $\mathbf{x}_i$ is two-dimensional with right singular vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2$ and singular values $\sigma_{i,1} = 10$, $\sigma_{i,2} = 1$. Neighbors of $\mathbf{x}_i$ extend ten times further along $\mathbf{v}_1$ than along $\mathbf{v}_2$:
\[
\text{$\mathbf{v}_1$ direction: long / fat / well-supported,} \qquad
\text{$\mathbf{v}_2$ direction: short / thin / fragile.}
\]
The whitened tangent coordinate of a neighbor offset $\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i$ along $\mathbf{v}_k$ is
$z_k = \langle \mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{v}_k \rangle / \sigma_{i,k}$. Moving 1 ambient unit along $\mathbf{v}_1$ is therefore $1/10 = 0.1$ \emph{whitened} units, while moving 1 ambient unit along $\mathbf{v}_2$ is $1/1 = 1$ whitened unit. Expressed in whitened coordinates the local neighborhood is approximately spherical: ``10\% of the whitened neighborhood'' is the same allowed move \emph{regardless of direction inside the tangent}. Translated back through the singular spectrum, that allowed move is large in ambient norm along $\mathbf{v}_1$ and small along $\mathbf{v}_2$, which is exactly the asymmetry we want: the linear approximation is reliable along high-$\sigma$ axes and fragile along low-$\sigma$ ones.

\paragraph{Comparison with the Euclidean radius.}
The Euclidean form is the inequality
\[
\| \boldsymbol{\delta} \|_2 \;\leq\; \varepsilon \cdot r_i,
\]
which treats every direction equally. The whitened form is approximately
\[
\Big\| \mathbf{B}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\delta} \,/\, \boldsymbol\sigma_i \Big\|_2 \;\leq\; \varepsilon \cdot R_i^{\mathrm{white}},
\]
where division is component-wise and $R_i^{\mathrm{spec}}$ is the median neighbour displacement in spectrally-normalized tangent coordinates (Eq.~\ref{eq:s_spectral}). Reading this inequality in plain English: \emph{after dividing each tangent coordinate of the edit by its local standard scale $\sigma_{i,k}$, the edit must be at most $\varepsilon$ times the typical neighbour displacement on that scale}. The same edit, projected onto a high-$\sigma$ axis, is allowed a larger ambient stride; projected onto a low-$\sigma$ axis, it is held to a much smaller stride.

\paragraph{Mental model.}
Imagine the local neighborhood as an ellipse stretched along its high-$\sigma$ axis and pinched along its low-$\sigma$ axis. A ``$\varepsilon$ Euclidean'' step ignores the ellipse shape: it steps the same length in any direction. A ``$\varepsilon$ spectrally-normalized'' step instead asks: \emph{after reshaping the ellipse into a sphere via $\sigma_{i,k}^{-1}$ scaling, does the edit fit inside an $\varepsilon$ ball?} The radius is then mapped back into the original representation space. The result is a direction-aware trust region: it prevents the iterative loop from taking a step that is numerically small in Euclidean norm but huge relative to a thin local tangent direction where the linear approximation is the least reliable.

This is the geometric reason why $\varepsilon = 0.1$ is interpretable as a smaller knob than a single global step size $\lambda$ would be: it bounds the edit relative to the local neighborhood's natural scale on each axis, not to a setting-independent constant.

\paragraph{Why $\varepsilon$ transfers across datasets without per-setting tuning.}
A direct consequence of the whitening is that $\varepsilon$ no longer carries the units of the local representation geometry: each tangent axis is rescaled by its own $\sigma_{i,k}$ before $\varepsilon$ is applied, so a representation setting with a wide local spread along its dominant axes and one with a narrow local spread both report the same allowed move when expressed as a fraction of their respective whitened neighborhoods. This is why we can fix $\varepsilon = 0.1$ once and use it across all 39 NLP settings (13 language models $\times$ 3 concepts at 50\%-depth) and all 80 CelebA settings (40 attributes $\times$ 2 control sets) without any per-setting tuning of the trust-region radius. Crucially, this transferability requires that the per-axis scale $\sigma_{i,k}$ \emph{itself} be estimated from the local representation, exactly what the local SVD on the neighborhood-secant matrix provides. A single global step size cannot have this property because its units are setting-specific: the same constant moves a Gemma-3-27B representation by a much larger ambient distance than it does a Qwen2.5-0.5B one.

\section{CelebA control-set construction (least- vs.\ most-correlated five attributes)}
\label{sec:appendix_celeba_controls}

For each of the 40 binary CelebA attributes used as a target concept, we report two complementary control regimes (Section~\ref{sec:celeba}, Table~\ref{tab:celeba_per_panel_breakdown}, Figure~\ref{fig:per_attr_bars_celeba}): the \emph{5 least-correlated} attributes and the \emph{5 most-correlated} attributes, both selected from the remaining 39 attributes. This appendix records exactly how the two control sets are computed.

\paragraph{Computation.}
Let $\mathbf{Y} \in \{0, 1\}^{N_{\text{train}} \times 40}$ be the train-split label matrix, with $\mathbf{Y}_{i,k}$ the label of training example $i$ on attribute $k$. We compute the empirical Pearson correlation matrix
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{R}_{k\ell} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(\mathbf{Y}_{:,k}, \mathbf{Y}_{:,\ell})}{\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{Y}_{:,k}) \cdot \mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{Y}_{:,\ell})}}
\quad \text{for } k, \ell \in \{1, \ldots, 40\}.
\end{equation}
For each target attribute $k$, we then rank the remaining $39$ attributes by absolute correlation $|\mathbf{R}_{k\ell}|$:
\begin{itemize}
\item the \emph{5 least-correlated} controls are the bottom 5 by $|\mathbf{R}_{k\ell}|$. They share \emph{minimal} information with the target and isolate clean surgicality.
\item the \emph{5 most-correlated} controls are the top 5 by $|\mathbf{R}_{k\ell}|$. They share \emph{substantial} information with the target and form a stricter test of whether erasure can preserve genuinely related downstream features.
\end{itemize}
The target attribute itself is excluded from the candidate pool (i.e., $\ell \neq k$). Pairs are computed in float64 with no shrinkage. 

\paragraph{Concrete examples.}
For \emph{Male}, the 5 most-correlated attributes are \emph{Wearing\_Lipstick} ($|r| \approx 0.78$), \emph{No\_Beard}, \emph{Heavy\_Makeup}, \emph{Attractive}, and \emph{5\_o\_Clock\_Shadow}; the 5 least-correlated are \emph{Narrow\_Eyes}, \emph{Blurry}, \emph{Straight\_Hair}, \emph{Receding\_Hairline}, and \emph{Pale\_Skin} (all $|r| < 0.05$). Erasing \emph{Male} while preserving the most-correlated controls is genuinely hard because the controls are partial proxies for the target; erasing while preserving the least-correlated controls tests whether the intervention is geographically narrow but not whether it disentangles correlated information. We therefore report both regimes side by side: each one isolates a different failure mode.

\paragraph{Why two regimes per concept.}
Reporting only one control set hides whichever regime the method happens to be weak in. A method that destroys the entire representation will pass the least-correlated test (controls are essentially independent of the edit anyway) and fail the most-correlated test catastrophically; conversely, a method that performs no erasure will pass both. The difference between the two regimes per method is therefore informative even when both individual numbers look acceptable. Section~\ref{sec:celeba} reads the difference as a measure of how much an erasure method confuses ``avoid this concept'' with ``avoid everything correlated with it''.

\section{Anisotropy regimes for the local-$\sigma$ spectrum}
\label{sec:appendix_alpha_regimes}

Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase_sigma} reweights each tangent coordinate $a_{i,k}$ by $\sigma_{i,k}^{\alpha}$, where $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$ is an anisotropy exponent and $\sigma_{i,k}$ are the singular values of the local secant matrix $\mathbf{S}_i$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:secant_svd}). Three non-negative values of $\alpha$ have natural interpretations:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$\alpha = 0$}: the update is isotropic in the tangent basis and reduces to Eq.~\ref{eq:tangent_erase}. Reported as an ablation.
\item \textbf{$\alpha = 1$}: each tangent direction is reweighted by $\sigma_{i,k}$. \textbf{This is the default in this paper.} Equivalently, the update applies the positive square root of the local secant covariance, $\mathbf{C}_i^{1/2}$, since the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{C}_i = \tfrac{1}{k} \mathbf{S}_i^\top \mathbf{S}_i$ are proportional to $\sigma_{i,k}^2$.
\item \textbf{$\alpha = 2$}: each tangent direction is reweighted by $\sigma_{i,k}^2$, equivalent to applying $\mathbf{C}_i$. This is the natural-gradient / full-Mahalanobis trust-region optimum: it is the first-order solution of $\min_{\boldsymbol\delta} \langle \mathbf{g}_i, \boldsymbol\delta \rangle$ subject to $\boldsymbol\delta^\top \mathbf{C}_i^{-1} \boldsymbol\delta \le \varepsilon^2$. Reported as an ablation; risks suppressing low-variance signal that may carry residual concept information.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Relation to Mahalanobis whitening (and what $\alpha = 1$ is \emph{not}).}
A reader familiar with Mahalanobis whitening might wonder whether our $\alpha = 1$ is the same operation. It is not. Mahalanobis whitening transforms the data so the covariance becomes identity, $\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{white}} = \mathbf{C}^{-1/2} \mathbf{x}$, and corresponds to scaling each tangent direction by $\sigma_{i,k}^{-1}$, i.e.\ $\alpha = -1$ in our notation. Whitening makes every direction equal-weighted, which would dilute the concept-specific signal we want to suppress and force every tangent axis to receive equal step magnitude.

We use a \emph{positive} power, $\sigma_{i,k}^{+1}$, which does the opposite: it reweights the tangent step \emph{toward} high-$\sigma$ principal axes (the directions where the local manifold has the most support and where the linear approximation is most reliable) and away from low-$\sigma$ thin tail directions where the linear approximation is fragile and motion most easily leaves the manifold. The three non-negative values $\alpha \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ form a one-parameter family interpolating between (i) treating every tangent direction as equally trustworthy ($\alpha = 0$, isotropic), (ii) reweighting by $\sigma_{i,k}$ to respect the local ellipsoid mildly ($\alpha = 1$, our default), and (iii) the natural-gradient / Mahalanobis trust-region optimum that maximally suppresses thin directions ($\alpha = 2$). Whitening ($\alpha = -1$) sits outside this range for our problem because its sign is opposite the geometric correction we want; we therefore do not include it.

\section{Coordinate-chart definition of the tangent space}
\label{sec:appendix_geometry}

We adopt the formal definition from \citet{tu-2011-manifolds}. Fix $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{M}$. A small local neighborhood $U \subset \mathcal{M}$ around $\mathbf{x}$ admits a coordinate map $\varphi : U \to \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^m$ with $\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$. The inverse $\varphi^{-1} : \Omega \to U \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is a local parametrization back into the ambient hidden space. The tangent space at $\mathbf{x}$ is the $m$-dimensional linear subspace
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M} = d(\varphi^{-1})_{0}(\mathbb{R}^m) \subset \mathbb{R}^d,
\end{equation}
where $d(\varphi^{-1})_0$ is the differential of $\varphi^{-1}$ at the origin (its Jacobian, in ML language). It maps an infinitesimal coordinate displacement in $\mathbb{R}^m$ to the corresponding first-order ambient displacement. Because $\mathcal{M}$ is embedded in $\mathbb{R}^d$, $T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M}$ is an $m$-dimensional linear subspace of the ambient hidden space.

In \TanGCE{} we never construct $\varphi$ or $\varphi^{-1}$. The leading right singular vectors of the local secant matrix (Eq.~\ref{eq:secant_svd}) serve as an empirical first-order basis for $T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M}$, and the orthogonal projector $\mathbf{P}_{T_i} = \mathbf{B}_i \mathbf{B}_i^\top$ implements the tangent component $\mathbf{g}^{\top}$ of any ambient vector $\mathbf{g}$. We do not claim that $\mathcal{M}$ is smooth in any theorem-level sense, only that this approximation tracks the local sample structure well enough to attenuate updates in directions unsupported by it.
