\section{Conclusion}

We introduced \TanGCE{}, the method presented in this paper: a manifold-aware concept erasure method that constrains erasure gradients to the local tangent space of the representation manifold. The central claim is not just that tangent projection removes more target information, but that it does so for the right geometric reason: when sentence representations occupy a much smaller natural manifold than the ambient hidden space, restricting updates to the tangent space lets the method use larger erasure strengths while keeping control-task changes small.

The comparison to IGBP and \AmbGCE{} clarifies the role of the manifold constraint. IGBP is the closest prior nonlinear baseline; \AmbGCE{} is the direct ablation with the same iterative loop but no tangent restriction; and \TanGCE{} is the full method. The resulting $\lambda$ sweep makes the practical benefit clear: ambient updates become unstable at large step sizes, whereas tangent-constrained updates continue to improve erasure precision.

Cross-modal experiments on 40 CelebA facial attributes in CLIP ViT-B/32 representations confirm that \TanGCE{} generalizes beyond language models. \TanGCE{} wins 36 of 40 attribute erasure tasks under fair HPO, reducing mean nonlinear leakage to 0.707 (vs.\ 0.839 for LEACE, the strongest baseline), with comparable control-task preservation. The remaining concepts are near-chance or favor another baseline.

More broadly, the iterative nonlinear protocol follows a conservative lesson from prior erasure work: if a freshly trained classifier can still recover the concept, then the concept is still present. By repeatedly retraining the scorer and constraining each erasure step to the tangent space, \TanGCE{} turns that lesson into a practical nonlinear erasure method.

Several directions remain open. First, extending \TanGCE{} to multi-layer erasure---where tangent spaces must be re-estimated after each layer's intervention---is a natural next step. Second, while our experiments use PCA-based tangent estimation, learned manifold representations (e.g., variational autoencoders) could provide more accurate tangent spaces. Finally, the connection between local geometric structure and erasure effectiveness suggests that manifold-aware methods may benefit other representation editing tasks beyond concept removal.
