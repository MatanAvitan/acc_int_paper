\section{Conclusion}

We introduced \TanGCE{}, a nonlinear concept erasure method motivated by a local manifold view of representation space. In this view, natural representations occupy a locally low-dimensional support inside a higher-dimensional ambient space, and local PCA/SVD provides a first-order approximation to the tangent space in a neighborhood. \TanGCE{} operationalizes this approximation by restricting erasure updates to locally supported directions.

Across evaluations spanning 13 language models across five families and a CLIP-based CelebA benchmark, \TanGCE{} is the stronger nonlinear remover than its ambient ablation, and the broader appendix comparisons show the same pattern against the earlier baselines included in the study. The geometry results support the manifold framing in a bounded sense: representations exhibit useful low-rank structure, and ambient-space updates can move them away from the support where the nonlinear concept is recovered. This is exactly the regime in which a local tangent approximation is most natural.

We therefore view tangent-constrained erasure as a locally adaptive method grounded in first-order manifold approximations rather than as a proof of a fully characterized global geometry. Several directions remain open. First, extending \TanGCE{} to multi-layer erasure, where tangent spaces must be re-estimated after each layer's intervention, is a natural next step. Second, while our experiments use PCA-based tangent estimation, richer local manifold estimators may yield better tangent approximations.
