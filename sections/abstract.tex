\section*{Abstract}

Although hidden states live in a high-dimensional ambient space, the representations used by natural inputs may lie near a much lower-dimensional local manifold. This motivates a geometric view of nonlinear concept erasure: if the relevant support is locally low-dimensional, then ambient updates can move in directions that are poorly supported by the local sample structure. We introduce Tangent Gradient-based Concept Erasure (\TanGCE{}), which approximates the local tangent space with neighborhood PCA and restricts erasure updates to that first-order local support. Across our completed text and vision evaluations, \TanGCE{} consistently reduces retrained nonlinear probe accuracy more than all prior methods, including its ambient ablation. Our geometry results support the manifold motivation in a bounded sense: representations exhibit useful low-rank local structure, and the relevant concept direction varies substantially across neighborhoods, so a single global erasure direction is often a poor local approximation. This is exactly the regime where a locally adaptive tangent-based method is expected to outperform a single global direction. We therefore position \TanGCE{} as a locally adaptive erasure method motivated by local manifold structure and implemented through first-order tangent approximations. More broadly, the connection between local geometric structure and intervention effectiveness suggests that manifold-aware methods may benefit other representation editing and optimization tasks beyond concept removal.
