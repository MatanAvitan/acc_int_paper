\subsection{Datasets, Models, and Compared Methods}

This subsection completes the experimental setup by describing the datasets, models, and comparison methods used to evaluate \TanGCE{}.

\subsubsection{Experimental Domains}
We evaluate our methods across five blocks:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sycophancy (Primary Nonlinear Case):} Our central focus is on removing agreement bias in model responses. We use the Anthropic model-written-evals sycophancy suite \citep{perez-etal-2022-model-written}, which comprises three subsets: NLP survey, PhilPapers 2020, and political typology quiz. Each example is a multiple-choice question prefaced by a persona bio that asserts a position, with a designated \emph{sycophantic} answer (the one matching the persona's assertion) and one or more \emph{non-sycophantic} alternatives. Because the dataset does not provide pre-assigned per-example concept labels, we assign them by running the model itself: concept~$=1$ if the model's argmax prediction matches the sycophantic answer, concept~$=0$ otherwise. This labelling strategy identifies exactly which examples activate the sycophancy direction in the model being studied, rather than relying on a fixed human annotation. For the surgicality control we use \emph{answer matching}: whether the model selects the non-sycophantic (factually correct) answer. This adapts the standard helpfulness control from safety datasets to the sycophancy setting, where ``helpful'' behaviour is operationalized as resisting social pressure and answering correctly.

    \item \textbf{Safety (Secondary Case Study):} We evaluate the surgicality of erasure on content safety using the PKU-SafeRLHF dataset \citep{ji-etal-2024-pku-saferlhf}, testing the preservation of helpfulness alongside safety leakage.
    \item \textbf{Bias in Bios (Diagnostic Baseline):} We use the Bias in Bios dataset \citep{dearteaga-etal-2019-bias} for gender and profession classification as a replication anchor. This setting provides a linear comparison point to contrast against the nonlinear gains of tangent-constrained erasure.
    \item \textbf{CelebA Visual Attributes (Cross-Modal Generalization):} We apply the same erasure methods to CLIP ViT-B/32 \citep{radford-etal-2021-clip} pooled embeddings (768 dimensions) of CelebA \citep{liu-etal-2015-celeba} face images. CelebA provides 40 binary facial attributes, enabling a rich surgicality analysis: when erasing one attribute (e.g., \emph{Male}), the remaining 39 serve as potential controls. We select the 5 least-correlated attributes as controls per concept to ensure that control accuracy changes reflect genuine collateral damage rather than attribute correlation.
    \item \textbf{FairFace Demographic Attributes (Cross-Dataset Vision Replication):} We additionally evaluate on FairFace \citep{karkkainen2021fairface}, using CLIP ViT-B/32 pooled embeddings for gender, race, and age concepts. This benchmark tests whether the vision-domain pattern observed on CelebA survives on a more demographically balanced dataset rather than a celebrity-image slice.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Models}
\paragraph{Language models.} The main seven-method comparison is run on Qwen2.5-0.5B \citep{qwen-2024-qwen25} (24 decoder layers, hidden dimension 896) at layer~8. To test whether the tangent-vs.-ambient gap persists across families and scales, we additionally compare \TanGCE{} and \AmbGCE{} on Qwen2.5-1.5B, Qwen2.5-3B, Gemma-2-2B, and Mistral-7B-v0.1, again at layer~8.

\paragraph{Vision encoder.} The CelebA and FairFace experiments use CLIP ViT-B/32 pooled embeddings (768 dimensions), extracted once and cached. We keep the encoder fixed across the two vision benchmarks so that the comparison isolates dataset effects rather than encoder differences.

\subsubsection{Compared Methods}
\paragraph{Methods.} The core quantitative comparison evaluates \TanGCE{} against six baselines and ablations: two random baselines (global and per-sample direction), linear projection, INLP \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null}, LEACE \citep{belrose-etal-2023-leace}, and \AmbGCE{}. IGBP \citep{iskander-etal-2023-shielded} is the closest prior nonlinear baseline and serves as the main methodological reference point for the GCE family: it iteratively trains two-class MLP probes and applies Newton-step boundary corrections. \AmbGCE{} is our direct ablation of \TanGCE{}: it uses the same scorer-training loop but applies the update in the full ambient space. \TanGCE{} adds the key ingredient of the paper by estimating local tangent spaces with PCA-based neighborhoods and restricting the erasure direction to a local on-manifold approximation rather than the full ambient space.

\paragraph{Comparison method definitions.}
All erasure methods share a common goal: given representations $\mathbf{x}_i$ that encode concept $\phi$, produce modified representations $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$ from which $\phi$ cannot be recovered, while preserving information unrelated to $\phi$.

\paragraph{Linear projection.}
Linear projection removes a single concept direction, typically estimated from the class-conditional means. It is the simplest global linear baseline and cannot remove nonlinear residual structure.

\paragraph{INLP (Iterative Nullspace Projection).}
INLP \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null} extends linear projection by repeatedly training linear classifiers on the residual and removing newly exposed linear directions. It is the canonical iterative linear baseline.

\paragraph{LEACE (Least-squares Concept Erasure).}
LEACE \citep{belrose-etal-2023-leace} is the strongest closed-form linear baseline in our comparisons. It performs a minimal linear intervention while eliminating linearly decodable concept information.

\paragraph{IGBP (Iterative Gradient-Based Projection).}
IGBP \citep{iskander-etal-2023-shielded} is the closest prior nonlinear baseline. It iteratively trains a nonlinear probe and moves each representation toward the current decision boundary by a Newton-style correction.

\paragraph{\AmbGCE{}.}
\AmbGCE{} is the direct ambient-space ablation of \TanGCE{}. It keeps the same nonlinear scorer loop but applies the update in the full hidden space rather than projecting it onto a local tangent approximation.
