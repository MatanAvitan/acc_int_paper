\section{Problem Setup and Geometric Background}
\label{sec:notation}

We first define the representation-editing problem, then introduce the geometric language used throughout the paper. Our notation follows the manifold viewpoint in Tu's introduction to smooth manifolds \citep{tu-2011-manifolds}: we use local coordinate maps, tangent spaces, and differentials only as local approximations, not as theorem-level guarantees about neural representations.

\paragraph{Language model and encoder.}
Let $\Sigma^*$ denote the set of all strings over a vocabulary $\Sigma$. A language model $p$ defines a distribution over $\Sigma^*$. We write $\mathrm{enc}_\ell : \Sigma^* \to \mathbb{R}^d$ for the hidden representation at layer $\ell$, where $d$ is the hidden dimension. For a fixed layer $\ell$ (omitted when clear from context), the representation of string $s$ is $\mathbf{x} = \mathrm{enc}_\ell(s) \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

\paragraph{Concepts and data.}
A binary concept is defined by a labeling function $\phi : \Sigma^* \to \mathcal{C}$, where $\mathcal{C} = \{0,1\}$. Each sample $i$ has representation $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and concept label $y_i = \phi(s_i) \in \mathcal{C}$, with an optional control label $z_i$ (e.g., profession for gender erasure). We write $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ for the dataset and $\mathcal{D}_c = \{i : y_i = c\}$ for the class-$c$ subset, with $N_c = |\mathcal{D}_c|$.

The class-conditional representation is the random variable $X_c \sim \mathrm{enc}_\ell(s) \mid \phi(s) = c$, i.e., the distribution of hidden states for strings labeled $c$.

\paragraph{Concept scorer.}
A concept scorer $f_\theta : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ is a two-layer MLP trained with binary cross-entropy on $\mathcal{D}$ to predict $y$ from $\mathbf{x}$. Its per-sample gradient with respect to the input is $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i) \in \mathbb{R}^d$, which points in the direction of steepest increase in concept probability at $\mathbf{x}_i$.

\paragraph{Representation manifold.}
Each layer representation $\mathbf{x} = \mathrm{enc}_\ell(s)$ is formally a point in the ambient hidden space $\mathbb{R}^d$, where $d$ is the full hidden-state dimension. Following Tu \citep{tu-2011-manifolds}, we model the representations that actually occur for real inputs as lying near a smooth embedded submanifold $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. The manifold $\mathcal{M}$ is our mathematical model of the representation manifold. Its intrinsic dimension is denoted by $m$, with $m < d$ and typically much smaller than $d$ in our settings, meaning that the local degrees of freedom used by the data are far fewer than the directions available in the ambient hidden space.

Now fix a point $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{M}$. Let $U \subset \mathcal{M}$ be a small local neighborhood around $\mathbf{x}$, and let
\begin{equation}
\varphi : U \to \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^m
\end{equation}
be a local coordinate map. Here $\Omega$ is an open set in the coordinate space $\mathbb{R}^m$. Intuitively, $\varphi$ assigns $m$ local coordinates to nearby points on the manifold. We choose the coordinates so that $\varphi(\mathbf{x}) = 0$, meaning that the point $\mathbf{x}$ is represented by the origin in local coordinates. The inverse map $\varphi^{-1} : \Omega \to U \subset \mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ is the corresponding local parametrization back into the ambient hidden space.

The tangent space at $\mathbf{x}$ is the best local linear approximation to the manifold near $\mathbf{x}$. Formally, it is the $m$-dimensional linear space
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_space_chart}
T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M} = d(\varphi^{-1})_{0}(\mathbb{R}^m) \subset \mathbb{R}^d,
\end{equation}
where $d(\varphi^{-1})_0$ is the differential of the local parametrization $\varphi^{-1}$ at the origin. In ordinary ML language, this differential can be read as the Jacobian or best local linear approximation of $\varphi^{-1}$ at $0$. It maps an infinitesimal coordinate displacement in $\mathbb{R}^m$ to the corresponding first-order displacement in the ambient space $\mathbb{R}^d$. Because $\mathcal{M}$ is embedded in $\mathbb{R}^d$, we can treat $T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M}$ as an $m$-dimensional linear subspace of the ambient hidden space.

\paragraph{Ambient versus tangent directions.}
For any ambient vector $\mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ attached to the point $\mathbf{x}$, the local geometric picture suggests a decomposition
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tangent_normal_decomp}
\mathbf{g} = \mathbf{g}^{\top} + \mathbf{g}^{\perp},
\qquad
\mathbf{g}^{\top} \in T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M},
\quad
\mathbf{g}^{\perp} \perp T_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{M}.
\end{equation}
Here $\mathbf{g}^{\top}$ is the tangent component, namely the part of $\mathbf{g}$ that moves along the manifold, and $\mathbf{g}^{\perp}$ is the normal component, namely the part that points away from the manifold. In this local approximation, the tangent component changes the representation along directions already available in natural data, while the normal component is the first-order source of off-manifold displacement. This is the geometric distinction used later by TanGCE: ambient gradient updates may contain both parts, but tangent-projected updates keep only the component that remains aligned with the local geometry of plausible representations.

\paragraph{Recoverability after erasure.}
Throughout the paper, concept removal is judged only after training a \emph{fresh} probe on the edited representations. This follows the lesson of \citet{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}: erasure succeeds only if a newly trained classifier still fails to recover the concept from $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$.

\paragraph{Conventions.}
Index $i$ ranges over samples.
$\|\cdot\|$ denotes the Euclidean norm unless stated otherwise.

\subsection{Baselines and Ambient Gradient Editing}
\label{subsec:baselines}

All erasure methods share a common goal: given representations $\mathbf{x}_i$ that encode concept $\phi$, produce modified representations $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i$ from which $\phi$ cannot be recovered, while preserving information unrelated to $\phi$. We review the linear baselines and the ambient nonlinear baselines before introducing the tangent-constrained method in Section~\ref{sec:method}.

\paragraph{Linear projection.}
Given a concept direction $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (typically the difference of class-conditional means, $\mathbf{w} = \mathbb{E}[X_1] - \mathbb{E}[X_0]$), linear projection removes the component along $\mathbf{w}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:linear_proj}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = (\mathbf{I} - \hat{\mathbf{w}}\hat{\mathbf{w}}^\top)\mathbf{x}_i, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{w} / \|\mathbf{w}\|.
\end{equation}
This removes a single linear direction but leaves all other concept-correlated structure intact.

\paragraph{INLP (Iterative Nullspace Projection).}
INLP \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null} iteratively trains linear classifiers $\mathbf{w}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{w}_T$ and projects onto the intersection of their nullspaces:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inlp}
\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{INLP}} = \prod_{t=1}^{T} (\mathbf{I} - \hat{\mathbf{w}}_t \hat{\mathbf{w}}_t^\top), \qquad \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{INLP}} \, \mathbf{x}_i.
\end{equation}
Each $\mathbf{w}_t$ is trained on the residual after projecting out $\mathbf{w}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{w}_{t-1}$. INLP extends linear projection to multiple directions but remains limited to linearly decodable concept information.

\paragraph{LEACE (Least-squares Concept Erasure).}
LEACE \citep{belrose-etal-2023-leace} finds an oblique projection $\mathbf{P} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ of rank $|\mathcal{C}|-1$ and a translation $\mathbf{b}$ that minimally modify representations while ensuring no log-linear classifier can recover the concept:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:leace}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{b}.
\end{equation}
LEACE guarantees $\mathbb{E}[\tilde{X}_c] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ and $\mathrm{Var}[\tilde{X}_c] = \mathrm{Var}[\tilde{X}_{c'}]$ for all $c, c' \in \mathcal{C}$, erasing both mean and covariance differences between class-conditional distributions. This provides the strongest linear erasure guarantee among the baselines.

\subsection{Gradient-Based Concept Erasure}
\label{subsec:gce}

The linear baselines above remove linearly decodable concept information but leave nonlinear structure intact. Several methods address this by computing per-sample erasure directions from the gradient of a nonlinear concept scorer.

\paragraph{IGBP (Iterative Gradient-Based Projection).}
IGBP \citep{iskander-etal-2023-shielded} trains a two-class MLP probe $g_\phi : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^2$ with softmax output, then retracts each representation to the decision boundary via a Newton step. Let $p_i = \max_c \operatorname{softmax}(g_\phi(\mathbf{x}_i))_c$ be the maximum class probability. IGBP uses the \emph{projection loss} $L_i = \tfrac{1}{2}\operatorname{logit}(p_i)^2$ and computes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:igbp}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \mathbf{x}_i - \frac{\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} L_i}{\|\boldsymbol{\theta}_i\|^2}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta}_i = \frac{\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} L_i}{\operatorname{logit}(p_i)},
\end{equation}
where $\operatorname{logit}(p) = \log\frac{p}{1-p}$. This is a Newton step that projects $\mathbf{x}_i$ onto the level set $\operatorname{logit}(p_i) = 0$ (i.e., $p_i = 0.5$, the decision boundary). Within each round, IGBP is therefore a one-shot boundary correction for the current scorer. The procedure then iterates: after projection, a fresh probe is trained on the projected representations and the process repeats until leakage falls to majority accuracy. Unlike Ambient GCE (below), IGBP has no explicit $\lambda$ parameter---the step size is determined by each sample's distance to the decision boundary.

\paragraph{Ambient GCE.}
Ambient GCE is the tangent-free ablation used later to isolate the manifold constraint: instead of moving directly to the current decision boundary, it takes a tunable $\lambda$-scaled step along the scorer gradient. Given a trained scorer $f_\theta$, the ambient erasure direction at sample $i$ is the unit gradient:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ambient_direction}
\mathbf{u}_i = \frac{\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i)}{\|\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i)\|}.
\end{equation}
The erased representation is obtained by projecting out the concept component:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ambient_erase}
\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_i = \mathbf{x}_i - \lambda \, (\mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_i) \, \mathbf{u}_i,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda > 0$ controls the erasure strength. At $\lambda = 1$, this removes the full projection onto $\mathbf{u}_i$; at $\lambda < 1$, it partially attenuates.

Ambient GCE edits in the full ambient space and therefore does not distinguish tangent from normal directions. Section~\ref{sec:method} introduces the tangent-constrained version of this update.
