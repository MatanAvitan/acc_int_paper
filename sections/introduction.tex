\section{Introduction}

Effective concept erasure in large language models requires a delicate balance between removing targeted information and preserving the model's broader utility. While gradient-based methods offer a path toward targeted intervention, they often suffer from substantial performance degradation. A natural hypothesis is that this collapse is driven in part by off-manifold edits: erasure directions computed in the high-dimensional ambient space can point orthogonal to the data manifold, displacing representations into low-density regions where the model's computation becomes unstable.

To address this failure mode, we propose Gradient-based Concept Erasure with Tangent projection (\texttt{gce\_tangent}). By constraining erasure gradients to the local tangent space of the representation manifold, \texttt{gce\_tangent} aims to remove concept-specific structure while preserving more of the underlying distributional geometry. This approach contrasts with \texttt{gce\_ambient}, which applies raw gradients in the full hidden space and therefore provides a direct baseline for the value of tangent projection.

Our contributions are threefold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{TanGCE: manifold-aware concept erasure.} We introduce Tangent Gradient-based Concept Erasure (TanGCE), which constrains erasure gradients to the local tangent space of the representation manifold. By retaining the tangent-projected gradient norm as a per-sample confidence weight, TanGCE naturally attenuates erasure for samples whose concept signal is predominantly off-manifold. We demonstrate that TanGCE achieves the lowest nonlinear leakage across three concept domains---sycophancy, gender, and safety---while preserving downstream task performance.

    \item \textbf{Manifold-aware mutual information evaluation.} We adopt a $k$-nearest-neighbor MI estimator that measures concept information through local neighborhood structure on the representation manifold. The estimator computes $I(\mathbf{X}; y)$ by examining, for each point, how many cross-class neighbors fall within the distance to its $k$-th same-class neighbor---a purely geometric quantity that captures all information (linear and nonlinear) without assuming a probe architecture. This creates a coherent geometric framework: TanGCE uses local PCA neighborhoods to estimate tangent spaces for erasure, while the $k$-NN MI estimator uses local ball neighborhoods to estimate information content. Both operate through local neighborhood structure rather than global ambient-space assumptions, and both degrade gracefully when the manifold assumption is only approximately satisfied. We compute MI for both concept labels (should decrease after erasure) and control labels (should remain unchanged), yielding a complete picture of erasure selectivity.

    \item \textbf{Unsupervised SAE-based erasure diagnostics.} We propose three metrics computed via sparse autoencoders trained on a general-purpose corpus (no concept labels needed): \emph{concept feature removal rate} (fraction of concept-correlated SAE features disrupted), \emph{collateral damage rate} (fraction of non-concept features disrupted), and \emph{feature redistribution score} (fraction of previously non-concept features that become concept-correlated after erasure). These metrics measure the surgicality and precision of erasure without requiring annotated control-task labels, and reveal failure modes invisible to downstream probes---including the critical finding that ambient erasure at high $\lambda$ \emph{creates} new concept correlations through off-manifold displacement (Section~\ref{sec:lambda_sweep}).
\end{enumerate}

We evaluate on sycophancy as the primary nonlinear concept, gender (Bias in Bios) as a diagnostic baseline, and safety as a secondary case study in Qwen2.5-0.5B at layer~8, and on 40 binary facial attributes in CLIP ViT-B/32 representations of CelebA to demonstrate cross-modal generalization. Under a fair validation-based hyperparameter protocol---where all methods search the same $\lambda$ grid and select by lowest validation nonlinear probe accuracy---TanGCE achieves the lowest nonlinear leakage across all domains while maintaining near-zero feature redistribution and preserving control-task accuracy. On CelebA, TanGCE wins 37 of 40 attribute erasure tasks, reducing mean nonlinear leakage to 0.707 (vs.\ 0.839 for LEACE), enabled by its ability to safely scale to $\lambda^*{=}8.0$ where ambient methods cannot.
