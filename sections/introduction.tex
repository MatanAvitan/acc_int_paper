\section{Introduction}

Effective concept erasure in large language models requires a delicate balance between removing targeted information and preserving the model's broader utility. While gradient-based methods offer a path toward surgical intervention, they often suffer from significant performance degradation. Our central argument is that this collapse is primarily driven by off-manifold edits. Erasure directions computed in the high-dimensional ambient space frequently point orthogonal to the data manifold, displacing representations into low-density regions where the model's computation becomes unstable.

To address this failure mode, we propose Gradient-based Concept Erasure with Tangent projection (\texttt{gce\_tangent}). By constraining erasure gradients to the local tangent space of the representation manifold, \texttt{gce\_tangent} removes concept-specific structure while maintaining the integrity of the underlying data distribution. This approach contrasts with \texttt{gce\_ambient}, which applies raw gradients in the full hidden space, often at the cost of representation fidelity.

Our investigation focuses on sycophancy as the primary nonlinear concept, representing a critical challenge for model alignment and safety. Bias in Bios (gender) serves as a diagnostic baseline to compare our manifold-aware approach against traditional linear erasure settings. Using Qwen2.5-0.5B as our experimental platform, we evaluate these methods across multiple layers to characterize the relationship between manifold geometry and erasure effectiveness. Results demonstrate that \texttt{gce\_tangent} consistently outperforms ambient baselines by minimizing off-manifold displacement, establishing manifold-aware erasure as a necessary framework for surgical and effective concept removal.
