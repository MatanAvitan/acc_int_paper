\section{Introduction}

Neural representations encode many attributes of their inputs: semantic content, style, demographic information, task labels, and other latent factors. In many applications, we may want to remove one such attribute, for example a protected or spurious concept (e.g., gender and toxicity), while preserving everything else the representation contains \citep{dearteaga-etal-2019-bias,avitan-etal-2025-practical}. This is the central tension in \textit{concept erasure}: removing the target information while unnecessarily damaging other control concepts, known as \textit{surgicality} \cite{singh-ravfogel-2024-representation-surgery}.

The surgicality requirement makes erasure especially difficult. A target concept is rarely encoded in isolation. It may be entangled with many other factors, most of which are unlabeled and therefore cannot be explicitly protected. Moreover, erasure must address \emph{recoverability}: even when a chosen classifier is driven to chance, a freshly trained classifier on the edited representations may still recover the protected attribute \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}. Linear erasure methods provide simple and effective closed-form interventions \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null,belrose-etal-2023-leace}, but they only remove linearly accessible information and do not generally block nonlinear probes \citep{ravfogel-etal-2022-adversarial}. Nonlinear erasure, however, introduces a different danger: if we directly follow the gradient of a nonlinear concept scorer in the ambient representation space, the edit may remove target signal by moving the representation to a region that natural inputs never occupy.

While neural representations live in $\mathbb{R}^d$, learned representations that are produced by different models do not fill this space uniformly. Instead, they concentrate near a much smaller, structured support that we treat as a low-dimensional \emph{manifold} embedded in the ambient space. This observation changes the geometry of erasure: an arbitrary direction in $\mathbb{R}^d$ may be a valid ambient vector, but it need not be tangent to the manifold of natural representations. Moving in such a direction pushes the representation \emph{off the manifold}, into a region the encoder does not normally produce, where downstream computations behave unpredictably and unrelated information is distorted. This is the central reason an off-manifold step damages \emph{unknown} control concepts: most of what the representation encodes is unlabeled, but every encoded concept --- labeled and unlabeled alike --- is read out by downstream layers from the same manifold. Pushing off the manifold corrupts that shared substrate; staying on it bounds the damage by construction. Linear erasure has a closed-form, low-rank solution that is applied uniformly to every representation: the operator targets a small number of global moments (a mean direction, a covariance asymmetry) and cannot adapt per sample. Nonlinear erasure has no analogous closed form. Without one, we must estimate per-sample directions of concept presence (typically as the gradient of a learned scorer) and apply them iteratively. The estimate has no built-in constraint to remain on the manifold, so a per-sample step that locally reduces concept signal can also push the representation off-manifold. Our central claim is that nonlinear erasure becomes surgical precisely when each edit is constrained to stay on the manifold of natural representations.

We introduce \TanGCE{}, a manifold-aware two-stage erasure method that operationalises this claim. Phase 1 is closed-form linear preprocessing: LEACE removes the first-moment mean-difference between target classes \citep{belrose-etal-2023-leace}, and a rank-2 projection removes the leading second-moment covariance asymmetry. Phase 2 is an iterative manifold-constrained loop. For each representation, we approximate the manifold locally by its first-order tangent space at that point, estimated via SVD on nearest-neighbor secants \citep{zhang-zha-2004-ltsa}. We project the concept-scorer gradient onto this tangent space, restricting the edit to directions the manifold actually extends along. We then rescale each tangent coordinate by the corresponding local singular value, a local-$\sigma$ preconditioner that ties the step magnitude in each direction to how far the manifold extends along that axis. The combined effect is that every nonlinear edit is an on-manifold step: target signal is removed, and the unlabeled control concepts that ride along the same manifold are protected by construction.

We test \TanGCE{} on text and vision tasks: 13 language models spanning 0.5B--27B parameters on three target concepts (sycophancy, gender bias, safety), and CLIP image embeddings on 40 face-attribute concepts under two control regimes. We operationalize surgicality on every setting by fixing a \emph{control-damage budget}, the maximum allowed drop in held-out control-concept probe accuracy, and reporting how much target-concept signal each method removes within it (measured by a fresh nonlinear probe). \TanGCE{} beats the strongest published baseline at every budget we test, under one fixed set of hyperparameters with no per-setting tuning.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We formulate nonlinear concept erasure as a manifold-constrained intervention problem: target signal should be removed by edits that stay on the local manifold of natural representations, so collateral damage to the many unlabeled control concepts encoded along that manifold is bounded by construction.
    \item We introduce \TanGCE{}, a manifold-aware two-stage erasure method combining closed-form low-order preprocessing with tangent-constrained nonlinear erasure.
    \item We propose a local-$\sigma$ preconditioner that scales tangent-coordinate steps by the local singular spectrum, making the trust region sensitive to the manifold's anisotropic geometry.
    \item We perform the most extensive nonlinear concept erasure evaluation to date, across 13 language models, three NLP target concepts, and 40 CelebA face attributes under two control regimes; \TanGCE{} achieves stronger target removal and better preservation of both labeled and unlabeled control concepts than the strongest baselines.
\end{enumerate}
