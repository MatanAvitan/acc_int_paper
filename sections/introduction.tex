Concerns about demographic bias in learned representations have motivated methods for identifying, interpreting, and removing protected information from neural activations \citep{dearteaga-etal-2019-bias,avitan-etal-2025-practical}. This paper focuses on the removal side of that problem: given fixed representations, the goal is to reduce recoverable target-concept information while preserving unrelated information.

Recent studies have primarily examined linear concept erasure \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null,belrose-etal-2023-leace}, where the goal is to remove linearly decodable concept information from a representation. But nonlinear concept erasure remains unresolved: kernelized adversarial erasure can block a chosen nonlinear adversary, yet protection need not transfer to other nonlinear adversaries \citep{ravfogel-etal-2022-adversarial}. This leaves nonlinear concept erasure as an important open problem for representation-level bias mitigation and related interventions.

We approach this vulnerability through a local manifold view of representation space. Hidden states live in a high-dimensional space, but the representations used by natural inputs need not fill it; instead, they may concentrate near a much smaller local support. Because unconstrained edits have the freedom to push representations off this natural support, they can distort independent features while leaving the target concept recoverable.

This suggests a different design principle for nonlinear erasure: rather than editing along the full gradient, we estimate a local tangent approximation around each sample and retain only the component of the update supported by that local geometry. In practice, we approximate the tangent space with local PCA on neighborhood secants. This is a first-order approximation rather than a theorem about hidden-state geometry, but it yields a concrete and testable erasure method.

We therefore evaluate with a recoverability-oriented protocol: after erasure, we retrain a fresh nonlinear probe and iterate the intervention to remove residual signal. Across evaluations spanning 13 language models across five families and a CLIP-based CelebA benchmark, \TanGCE{} is the stronger nonlinear remover than its ambient ablation and the earlier methods we compare against. The geometry results support the manifold motivation in a bounded form: representations show local low-rank structure, but ambient edits can still leave the natural support.
