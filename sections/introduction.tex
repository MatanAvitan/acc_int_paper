\section{Introduction}

Effective concept erasure must do two things at once: remove the target concept and preserve the rest of the representation. This is especially difficult for nonlinear concepts: an edit may defeat one trained probe while leaving behind signal that a freshly trained nonlinear classifier can still recover, as emphasized by Elazar and Goldberg's cautionary analysis of post-hoc recovery after adversarial removal \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}. For erasure to be useful, it must therefore achieve two goals simultaneously: high \emph{erasure precision} on the target concept and high \emph{surgicality} on control concepts.

We approach this problem through a local manifold view of representation space. Hidden states live in a high-dimensional ambient space, but the representations used by natural inputs need not fill that space. Instead, they may concentrate near a much smaller local support. Under this view, two geometric difficulties arise for nonlinear erasure. First, an ambient update can move in directions that are weakly supported by the local geometry. Second, even when a useful low-rank global approximation exists, a single global direction may still be a poor summary of the concept in a neighborhood.

This suggests a different design principle for nonlinear erasure. Rather than editing along the full ambient gradient, we estimate a local tangent approximation around each sample and retain only the component of the update supported by that local geometry. In practice, we approximate the tangent space with local PCA/SVD on neighborhood secants. This is a first-order approximation rather than a theorem about hidden-state geometry, but it yields a concrete and testable erasure method.

Our contributions are twofold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A manifold-motivated formulation of nonlinear concept erasure.} We frame nonlinear erasure through a local manifold hypothesis: the relevant question is not only whether representations are low-rank, but whether a single global direction is sufficient or whether local charts are needed.

    \item \textbf{A tangent-constrained nonlinear erasure method.} In Section~\ref{sec:method}, we introduce \TanGCE{}, which operationalizes this view by projecting ambient erasure updates onto locally estimated tangent spaces so that interventions follow the first-order geometry supported by nearby representations.
\end{enumerate}

To evaluate the method conservatively, we adopt a recoverability-oriented protocol following Elazar and Goldberg's cautionary analysis \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial} and the iterative perspective behind INLP \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null}: we do not treat success against a single scorer as evidence of complete erasure, we evaluate with freshly retrained nonlinear probes, and we iterate the erasure procedure to remove residual recoverable signal. Across our completed text and vision evaluations, \TanGCE{} is the stronger nonlinear remover than its ambient ablation, and all previous concept erasure methods. The geometry results support the manifold motivation in a bounded form: representations exhibit low-rank local structure, but the primary nonlinear case is poorly summarized by a single global direction. Section~\ref{sec:related} positions the method against prior erasure work, Section~\ref{sec:method} gives the method and modeling view, Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} gives the experimental setup, and Section~\ref{sec:results} evaluates both erasure performance and the geometry motivating local tangent constraints.
