\section{Introduction}

Neural representations encode many attributes of their inputs: semantic content, style, demographic information, task labels, and other latent factors. In many applications, we may want to remove one such attribute, for example a protected or spurious concept, while preserving everything else the representation contains \citep{dearteaga-etal-2019-bias,avitan-etal-2025-practical}. This is the central tension in \textit{concept erasure}: removing the target information while unnecessarily damaging other control concepts, known as \textit{sergicality}.

This requirement makes erasure especially difficult. A target concept is rarely encoded in isolation. It may be entangled with many other factors, most of which are unlabeled and therefore cannot be explicitly protected. Moreover, erasure must address \emph{recoverability}: even when a chosen classifier is driven to chance, a freshly trained classifier on the edited representations may still recover the protected attribute \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}. Linear erasure methods provide simple and effective closed-form interventions \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null,belrose-etal-2023-leace}, but they only remove linearly accessible information and do not generally block nonlinear probes \citep{ravfogel-etal-2022-adversarial}. Nonlinear erasure, however, introduces a different danger: if we directly follow the gradient of a nonlinear concept scorer in the ambient representation space, the edit may remove target signal by moving the representation to a region that natural inputs never occupy.

Our starting point is that hidden states live in $\mathbb{R}^d$, but natural representations do not fill this space uniformly. Instead, they concentrate near a much smaller, structured support: the region of representation space that is actually reachable from natural inputs. This observation changes the geometry of erasure. An arbitrary direction in the ambient space may be a valid vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$, but it need not be a valid direction along the representation support. Moving in such a direction can push the representation off-support, distorting unrelated information and harming control concepts. We therefore ask whether nonlinear erasure can be made more surgical by forcing the edit to remain locally compatible with the geometry of natural representations.

We introduce \TanGCE{}, a tangent gradient-based method for nonlinear concept erasure. Around each representation, we estimate a local tangent space using the SVD of nearest-neighbor secants. Instead of applying the ambient gradient of a concept scorer, we project that gradient onto the estimated tangent space and edit only along directions supported by the local neighborhood. This constrains erasure to directions that are locally plausible under the \emph{representation manifold}, reducing the risk of off-support interventions.

A second ingredient is needed for stability. The local tangent space is not isotropic: different tangent directions correspond to different local scales of variation. A step of the same Euclidean length can be harmless along a high-variance direction but destructive along a low-variance one. We therefore use the singular spectrum produced by the same local SVD to precondition the tangent step. After projecting the scorer gradient onto the tangent space, we express it in the local principal-coordinate system and rescale each coordinate by its corresponding local scale. This local-$\sigma$ preconditioner ties the magnitude of the intervention in each direction to how far the representation neighborhood actually extends in that direction, yielding a per-row trust region that transfers across models and layers.

Our full pipeline has two stages. First, we remove dominant low-order target signal in closed form. We apply LEACE to remove the first-order mean-difference direction between the two target classes \citep{belrose-etal-2023-leace}, and then remove a rank-2 subspace corresponding to the leading covariance-asymmetry directions of the class-conditional covariance difference. This sacrifices only a small number of representation dimensions while reducing the strongest linear and quadratic traces of the target concept. Second, we run tangent-constrained nonlinear erasure on the remaining signal using the local-$\sigma$ preconditioned tangent gradients described above.

The empirical signature of this design is surgicality. We evaluate \TanGCE{} on 119 panels under fixed pipeline hyperparameters, without per-panel tuning: 13 language models $\times$ 3 concepts at 50\%-depth, and CelebA-CLIP $\times$ 40 attributes $\times$ 2 control-set regimes. At a fixed control-damage budget of $\Delta Y \ge -5$pp, \TanGCE{} reaches the per-panel majority-class probe-accuracy floor on $33$ of $39$ NLP panels, compared with $22/39$ for Obliviator, while damaging the control task less; the same pattern holds at every other budget we test ($\le 1$pp, $\le 3$pp, $\le 10$pp).

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We formulate nonlinear concept erasure as a geometry-constrained intervention problem: target information should be removed without moving representations away from the local support of natural inputs.
    \item We introduce \TanGCE{}, a two-stage erasure pipeline combining closed-form low-order preprocessing with tangent-constrained nonlinear erasure.
    \item We propose a local-$\sigma$ preconditioner that scales tangent-coordinate steps by the local singular spectrum, making the trust region sensitive to the manifold's anisotropic geometry.
    \item We evaluate \TanGCE{} on 119 panels under a single fixed-$\varepsilon$ protocol, showing stronger target removal and control preservation than Obliviator across NLP and vision-language settings.
\end{enumerate}
