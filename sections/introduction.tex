\section{Introduction}

Effective concept erasure in neural representations requires a delicate balance between removing targeted information and preserving the rest of the representation. This is especially difficult for nonlinear concepts: an edit may defeat one trained probe while leaving behind signal that a freshly trained nonlinear classifier can still recover, as emphasized by Elazar and Goldberg's cautionary analysis of post-hoc recovery after adversarial removal \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}. For erasure to be useful, it must therefore achieve two goals simultaneously: high \emph{erasure precision} on the target concept and high \emph{surgicality} on control concepts.

We argue that a central reason for the utility collapse of ambient-space erasure is geometric. The hidden space of a language model can be high-dimensional, but the image of actual sentence representations need not fill that ambient Euclidean space. Instead, it can concentrate near a much lower-dimensional support. In our primary nonlinear setting, this effective dimension is roughly an order of magnitude smaller than the ambient one. When gradient-based erasure is applied in the full hidden space while the data occupy only this lower-dimensional support, the risk of stepping off the natural representation manifold increases. Once that happens, even a direction that is locally predictive of the target concept can damage unrelated structure simply by pushing the representation into regions the model does not normally occupy.

This observation suggests a different design principle for nonlinear erasure: the edit should follow the local geometry of the representation manifold rather than the full ambient space. Intuitively, if the relevant representation support is modeled locally by a tangent space, then only the tangent component of an erasure update preserves the first-order geometry actually used by the model. Constraining edits to that tangent geometry should make erasure more surgical and, crucially, should allow larger erasure strengths $\lambda$ before control concepts collapse.

Empirically, the resulting tangent-constrained method achieves the strongest nonlinear erasure across sycophancy, gender, safety, and CelebA visual attributes while maintaining competitive control-concept preservation. On CelebA, it wins 36 of 40 attribute erasure tasks, reducing mean nonlinear leakage to 0.707 (vs.\ 0.839 for LEACE), enabled by its ability to safely scale to larger $\lambda$ values where ambient methods cannot.
