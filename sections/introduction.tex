\section{Introduction}

Effective concept erasure in neural representations requires a delicate balance between removing targeted information and preserving the rest of the representation. This is especially difficult for nonlinear concepts: a method can appear to erase the concept for one classifier while still leaving behind recoverable information for a freshly trained one, as emphasized by Elazar and Goldberg's cautionary analysis of post-hoc recovery after adversarial removal \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}. For an erasure method to be useful, it must therefore achieve two goals simultaneously: high \emph{erasure precision} on the target concept and high \emph{surgicality} on control concepts.

A natural explanation for the usual utility collapse is geometric. The ambient hidden space of a language model can be very large, but the subset actually occupied by sentence representations is often much smaller; in our primary nonlinear setting, the effective representation dimension is roughly an order of magnitude below the ambient one. When gradient-based erasure is applied in the full hidden space while the data occupy only this lower-dimensional support, the probability of stepping off the natural manifold rises sharply. Once that happens, even a direction that is locally predictive of the target concept can damage unrelated structure simply by pushing the representation into regions the model does not normally occupy. This suggests that concept erasure should intervene \emph{on the manifold used by the model}, not in the full ambient space. Doing so should make the intervention more surgical and, crucially, should allow the method to use a larger erasure strength $\lambda$ before control concepts begin to collapse.

To operationalize this idea, we propose Gradient-based Concept Erasure with Tangent projection (TanGCE). TanGCE is the method presented in this paper. It constrains nonlinear erasure gradients to the local tangent space of the representation manifold, estimated from nearby examples, so that the intervention acts in the lower-dimensional geometry where sentence representations actually live. Ambient GCE is included only as the direct ablation without the tangent constraint, and IGBP \citep{iskander-etal-2023-shielded} serves as the closest prior nonlinear baseline. This framing isolates the core claim of the paper: the gain comes from the tangent-space restriction itself.

Our contributions are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{TanGCE: tangent-constrained nonlinear concept erasure.} We introduce TanGCE, a nonlinear concept erasure method that projects each erasure update onto a locally estimated tangent space so that the intervention follows the geometry of the representation manifold rather than the full ambient space.

    \item \textbf{A low-dimensional manifold motivation for surgical erasure.} We argue that the relevant failure mode of ambient nonlinear erasure is off-manifold intervention: sentence representations occupy a much smaller support than the ambient hidden space, so unrestricted gradient steps are likely to leave that support and create collateral damage. This viewpoint predicts that on-manifold erasure should remain more surgical and support larger $\lambda$ values.

    \item \textbf{Iterative nonlinear erasure motivated by recoverability.} Following the core insight behind Elazar and Goldberg's recovery result \citep{elazar-goldberg-2018-adversarial}, we do not treat success against a single trained scorer as evidence of complete removal. Instead, TanGCE retrains a fresh nonlinear scorer after each round, in the same spirit that iterative linear methods such as INLP \citep{ravfogel-etal-2020-null} repeatedly remove newly exposed residual information. Ambient GCE uses the same loop as the ablation.

    \item \textbf{A supervised evaluation centered on precision and surgicality.} We evaluate on three text datasets and one large vision benchmark, using retrained concept probes to measure erasure precision and control-concept performance to measure surgicality. Across sycophancy, gender, safety, and CelebA facial attributes, TanGCE consistently attains stronger nonlinear erasure than competing baselines while maintaining competitive control-concept preservation.
\end{enumerate}

We evaluate on sycophancy as the primary nonlinear concept, gender (Bias in Bios) as a diagnostic baseline, and safety as a secondary case study in Qwen2.5-0.5B at layer~8, and on 40 binary facial attributes in CLIP ViT-B/32 representations of CelebA to demonstrate cross-modal generalization. Under a fair validation-based hyperparameter protocol---where all $\lambda$-based methods search the same grid and select by lowest validation nonlinear probe accuracy---TanGCE achieves the lowest nonlinear leakage across all domains while preserving downstream control concepts. On CelebA, TanGCE wins 36 of 40 attribute erasure tasks, reducing mean nonlinear leakage to 0.707 (vs.\ 0.839 for LEACE), enabled by its ability to safely scale to larger $\lambda$ values where ambient methods cannot.
